Quick Recall from Lesson 1 🧠

GEOCENTRIC = Earth stays still, everything circles Earth (the old idea). HELIOCENTRIC = the Sun stays still, planets (including Earth) circle the Sun (the idea confirmed correct). For centuries, both models fit everyday sky observations equally well — nobody could prove which was right using only their eyes.
Today we go further: in 1610, a scientist named Galileo pointed a brand-new invention — the telescope — at Venus. What he saw became one of the strongest pieces of evidence ever found for the heliocentric model.

Why learn this? 🤔

SCIENTIST'S TOOL: when two ideas both seem to explain the same evidence equally well, look for a NEW observation where the two ideas make DIFFERENT predictions — then go check which prediction actually happens.
This is exactly what makes this story such a great science lesson: it's not just "Galileo looked through a telescope and saw Venus" — it's that BOTH models had already made a clear, different prediction about what he should see, before he even looked. Testing a prediction like this is one of the most powerful tools in all of science.

Venus Shows Phases Too 🌗

Just like the Moon, Venus has no light of its own — it only shines because it reflects sunlight. So Venus can show phases too: crescent, half, gibbous, or full.
Recall from Unit 7: the Moon's phase depends on how much of its sunlit half we can see from Earth, which changes as it orbits. Venus works the same way — its phase depends on how much of ITS sunlit half faces Earth, which changes as Venus moves along its own path.
The key question: would Venus's phases look different depending on whether the geocentric or heliocentric model is true? Yes — and that difference is exactly what makes this such powerful evidence.

Two Different Predictions ☀️🔭

GEOCENTRIC: Venus was pictured always staying close to the Sun's position in our sky, so it should only ever show a thin crescent — never a half, gibbous, or full phase.
HELIOCENTRIC: Venus orbits the Sun independently of Earth, so from Earth we should be able to see Venus at many different angles — meaning it should show the FULL range of phases, including gibbous and full.
The evidence: in 1610, Galileo pointed his telescope at Venus and saw it moving through crescent, half, gibbous, AND full phases — the full range. This matched the heliocentric prediction exactly, and did not match the geocentric prediction at all. This became one of the strongest pieces of observational evidence that the heliocentric model was correct.
