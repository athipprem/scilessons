Quick Recall from Unit 7 🔁

🔁 Astronomy check: back in Unit 7, you learned the Moon doesn't make its own light at all — it just reflects sunlight bouncing off its dusty surface, back to our eyes on Earth.

That single fact is actually the key idea behind today's whole lesson! Some things — like the Sun — truly make their own light. Everything else we see, including the Moon, this page, and your own hand, is only visible because light from somewhere else is bouncing off it and travelling into your eyes.

Why learn this? 🤔

Understanding how light travels and bounces explains why mirrors work, why your shadow has a shape, why eclipses happen, why photographers use big soft lights for gentler shadows, and even why submarines use periscopes to see around corners.
Real life: engineers design streetlights and car headlights around how far straight light rays can reach. Photographers and film crews use big broad lights (called "softboxes") specifically because they create softer, less harsh shadows than one small bulb. Astronomers use the umbra and penumbra of the Moon's shadow to predict exactly where a solar eclipse will be visible on Earth — you'll learn exactly what those two words mean in Lesson 2.

Luminous or Non-Luminous? 💡

A LUMINOUS object makes its OWN light. A NON-LUMINOUS object only becomes visible by REFLECTING light from somewhere else.
☀️ Natural luminous
Sun, stars, fire/flames
makes its own light, no help from people
💡 Man-made luminous
light bulbs, torches, candles
makes its own light, built or lit by people
🌙 Non-luminous (reflects)
Moon, planets, a mirror's image
visible only because light bounces off it
📖 Non-luminous (everyday)
books, hands, clothes, walls
visible only because light bounces off it
Key idea: "luminous" is about making light, not about how bright or shiny something looks. A mirror looks shiny, but a mirror doesn't make its own light — it's non-luminous, it just reflects very well.

Light Travels in Straight Lines ➡️

Light always travels in straight lines, called RAYS, until something blocks, bends, or bounces it.
Straight light ray from the bulb hits the object, then bounces (reflects) in a new straight line into the eye — that's how the object gets seen.
Key idea: this is why you can block light with your hand to make a shadow (the straight path gets interrupted), and why light bounces off a surface at an angle instead of curving around it.

Luminous or Not? 🔍

Ask this one key question about anything that reaches your eyes as light.
Does the object make its OWN light using energy stored inside it (electricity, burning, or heat) — rather than just bouncing light off its surface?
YES
It's LUMINOUS — a real light source, like the Sun, a lamp, or a candle flame.
NO
It's NON-LUMINOUS — it only becomes visible when light from a luminous source bounces off it into your eyes, like the Moon, a book, or your hand.
Key idea: don't be fooled by brightness or shininess — always ask whether the object is truly MAKING the light itself, or just reflecting light that started somewhere else.
