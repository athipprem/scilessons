Quick Recall from Unit 8 🌿

🔁 Photosynthesis check: in Unit 8 you learned that photosynthesis combines water and carbon dioxide, using sunlight energy, to make glucose (food) and oxygen.

Here's the connection: photosynthesis is actually a CHEMICAL REACTION! Brand new substances (glucose and oxygen) are formed from completely different starting ingredients (water and carbon dioxide) — that's exactly what a chemical reaction is. Today you'll learn how to spot reactions like this one, and many others, in everyday life.

Why learn this? 🤔

Chemical reactions are happening around you constantly — cooking food, a car engine burning fuel, a battery powering a torch, bread rising, and even your own body digesting lunch. Being able to tell a physical change from a chemical one, and spot the clues, is one of the most useful "explain what you see" skills in science.
Real life: firefighters know burning is a fast chemical reaction that needs fuel, heat, and oxygen — remove any one and the fire stops. Bakers rely on a chemical reaction (baking soda reacting with an acid) to make cakes rise. Farmers and builders protect iron and steel from rusting, because rust is a slow chemical reaction with oxygen and water that ruins metal over time.

Physical vs Chemical Change ⚖️

A PHYSICAL change alters how a substance LOOKS or its STATE, but it is still the same substance. A CHEMICAL change (a reaction) turns it into a genuinely NEW substance.
PHYSICAL change
melting ice, tearing paper, cutting wood, dissolving sugar
same substance, different look/state
CHEMICAL change (reaction)
burning wood, rusting iron, baking a cake, mixing vinegar + baking soda
brand new substance(s) form
Key idea: if you melt an ice cube, it's still water — you can freeze it back. If you burn a piece of paper, the ash and smoke are completely different substances from paper, and you can never turn ash back into paper. That's the real test: has a genuinely NEW substance been created?

The Clues: Signs of a Chemical Reaction 🫧

You usually can't see new substances forming directly, but these FOUR clues tell you a chemical reaction is probably happening.
🫧
Gas / Bubbles
a gas is being given off
🎨
Colour Change
the substance looks different
🌡️
Temperature Change
it gets hotter or colder
👃
New Smell
a different smell appears
Key idea: no single clue proves a reaction on its own (ice also gets cold when it melts, and that's just physical!), but they're useful warning signs. The real proof is always: did a genuinely new substance form? Vinegar and baking soda fizzing (gas + a new liquid forming) is a chemical reaction; an ice cube "fizzing" as it melts in soda is not — it's just releasing trapped gas physically.

Is This a Chemical Reaction? 🔍

Use this checklist to decide whether something you observe is a chemical reaction.
Did a genuinely NEW substance form, with different properties from what you started with?
↓
YES
This IS a chemical reaction
e.g. rusting, burning, baking soda + vinegar
NO — still the same substance
This is just a PHYSICAL change
e.g. melting, freezing, dissolving, tearing, cutting
Key idea: an iron nail left in damp air slowly turns to rust — a completely different substance from iron, so that's a chemical reaction. That same nail bent into a new shape is still iron, just a different shape — a physical change. Same starting material, two very different kinds of change.

3. An ice cube melts into a puddle of water. What kind of change is this?

✔ Exactly — melting is a physical change. It's still H2O; you could freeze it right back.
✘ Ask: is it still the very same substance afterwards? What substance is ice, and what substance is the puddle?

4. A piece of paper is burned, leaving behind ash and smoke. What kind of change is this?

✔ Correct — burning creates genuinely new substances (ash, smoke, gases) that can never be turned back into paper.
✘ Ask the key question: can you easily get the original paper back from the ash? What does that tell you?

5. Sugar is stirred into a cup of water until it disappears. What kind of change is this?

✔ Right — dissolving is physical. The sugar molecules are still sugar; evaporating the water would leave the sugar behind.
✘ Has the sugar turned into a genuinely different substance, or is it just spread out through the water?
