Two Maps for Today 🗺️

MAP 1 — Mould (and most microorganisms) need FOOD, MOISTURE (water) and WARMTH to grow — and most also need AIR.
MAP 2 — Scientist's tool: to find out which condition matters most, change ONE thing at a time and keep everything else the same.
🎯 By the end you can: say what conditions make mould grow fastest or slowest, and judge whether a mould experiment is a fair test. This is exactly the kind of question ASMOPSS asks!

Why learn this? 🤔

Ever wonder why bread goes mouldy on the kitchen counter in days, but lasts weeks in the freezer? Or why we keep leftovers in a sealed container in the fridge instead of an open bowl on the table? That's not a coincidence — it's you controlling the exact conditions mould needs to grow.
Real life: fridges slow mould down by removing warmth. Freezers stop it almost completely. Sealed, dry containers remove moisture. Bakers, on the other hand, deliberately give yeast (a helpful fungus) warmth, moisture and food so it grows FAST and makes bread rise. Your superpower: once you know what a microorganism needs, you can control whether it grows fast, slow, or not at all.

What Does Mould Need to Grow? 🍄

Mould is a fungus — a living thing. Like all living things, it needs the right conditions to survive and grow.
🍞
FOOD
something organic to feed on — bread, fruit, old leftovers
💧
MOISTURE
water/dampness — dry food resists mould far longer
🌡️
WARMTH
mould grows fastest in warm places, very slowly in the cold
Key fact: most moulds also need AIR to grow, which is why sealing food in an airtight container (as well as keeping it cool and dry) helps slow mould down even further.

Fast Growth vs Slow Growth 🔥🧊

MORE food + moisture + warmth = FASTER mould growth. LESS of any one of them = SLOWER growth.
GROWS MOULD FAST
warm room, damp/moist bread, exposed to air
GROWS MOULD SLOWLY
fridge/freezer (cold), dry bread, sealed away from air

Think Like a Scientist 🔬

FAIR TEST RULE: change ONE thing, measure ONE thing, keep everything else the SAME.

Imagine testing: "Does moisture make mould grow faster?" Four identical bread bags are set up like this:

BagBreadMoisturePlace kept
Asame size slicedrywarm cupboard
Bsame size slicedamp (few drops of water)warm cupboard
Csame size slicedryfridge
Dsame size slicedamp (few drops of water)fridge

To find out if MOISTURE ALONE makes a difference, compare Bag A with Bag B — both are kept warm, so the only thing that's different between them is moisture. If Bag B moulds faster than Bag A, moisture is the reason, not the warmth (since both were warm).

CHANGE
one thing
(moisture, when comparing A vs B)
MEASURE
one thing
(how much mould appears, and how fast)
SAME
everything else
(bread type & size, bag type, where kept, starting time)
🧠 Why comparing the RIGHT pair matters: comparing Bag B (damp, warm) with Bag C (dry, fridge) would NOT tell you about moisture on its own — two things changed at once (moisture AND temperature), so you couldn't be sure which one caused the difference. Always compare bags that differ in only ONE way.
