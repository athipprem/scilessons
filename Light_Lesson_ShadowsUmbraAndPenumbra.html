Quick Recall from Lesson 1 🔁

🔁 Lesson 1 check: light always travels in straight lines until something blocks or bends it.

So what happens when an object gets directly in the way of those straight light rays? The light can't bend around the object — it gets blocked, and a dark shape called a shadow forms on the surface behind it.

Why learn this? 🤔

Umbra and penumbra explain why solar eclipses are only total in a small path but partial over a much wider area, why cloudy-day shadows look softer than sunny-day shadows, why photographers pick certain lights on purpose, and why a sundial can tell the time from a shadow's edge.
Real life: during a solar eclipse, people standing in the Moon's umbra see the Sun completely blocked, while people in the much larger penumbra only see a partial eclipse. Film and photography studios choose between small "hard" lights and big "soft" lights on purpose, depending on whether they want crisp, dramatic shadows or gentle, flattering ones.

How Shadows Form 🌑

Only OPAQUE materials block light completely enough to cast a strong, dark shadow.
🪟 Transparent
lets almost ALL light through
clear glass, clean water
→ barely any shadow
🌫️ Translucent
lets SOME light through, scattered
frosted glass, tissue paper
→ faint, blurry shadow
🚫 Opaque
blocks ALL light completely
wood, metal, a book
→ strong, dark shadow
Key idea: a shadow can only form where light is stopped from reaching a surface. The more completely a material blocks light (opaque, most blocked), the darker and stronger its shadow will be.

Point Source vs Broad Source 🔦

A tiny, single-point light source makes a totally different shadow than a wide, broad light source — even with the exact same object and screen.
A POINT SOURCE (like a distant star, or a bulb far away) sends light rays from just one spot. Every part of the screen is either fully lit or fully blocked — so the shadow has one sharp, crisp edge. This whole dark shadow is called the UMBRA.
A BROAD (extended) SOURCE sends light rays from many different spots across its width. Right behind the object, NO light from ANY part of the source can get through — that narrow, fully-dark centre is still called the UMBRA. But just outside it, light from SOME parts of the source sneaks past — that partly-lit, softer-edged fringe is called the PENUMBRA.
Key idea: the wider (or closer) the light source is compared to the object, the bigger and fuzzier the penumbra fringe becomes — that's exactly why a long fluorescent tube makes softer-edged shadows than a small torch bulb.

Predict the Shadow 🔍

Ask this one question about the light source before predicting what its shadow will look like.
Is the light source small and far away, acting basically like a single POINT (e.g. a tiny star, or a bulb far across a room)?
YES
Expect a SHARP-edged shadow — just one UMBRA region, no penumbra, because light only travels from one place.
NO — it's broad/extended
Expect a shadow with a dark UMBRA centre AND a lighter, fuzzy PENUMBRA fringe around its edges.
Key idea: the actual physical size of the source doesn't matter as much as how big it LOOKS from the object's position — a huge source very far away can act almost like a point, while a small source held very close can act almost like a broad one.

Think Like a Scientist 🔬

WORKED EXAMPLE: predicting a pencil's shadow under a classroom light.
Scenario: A pencil is held just above a desk, directly under a long fluorescent classroom light tube.
  1. The fluorescent tube is a broad, extended light source — not a single point.
  2. Different parts along the tube's length send light rays from slightly different positions.
  3. Directly under the pencil, NO light from any part of the tube can get past — that region is the umbra, fully dark.
  4. Just to the sides of that darkest strip, light from SOME parts of the tube sneaks past the pencil while light from other parts is still blocked — that's the penumbra, a softer, partly-lit fringe.
  5. Prediction: the pencil's shadow on the desk will show a dark central stripe (umbra) fading into a lighter, blurrier edge (penumbra) — not one perfectly sharp line.
Spot the flawed claim: a student says "a broad light source always makes a SHARPER shadow than a point source, because it sends out more total light." Is this correct? (No — sending out more total light doesn't make edges sharper. A broad source actually makes shadows SOFTER, with more penumbra, because it lights the object from many different angles at once. A point source, even a very dim one, gives the sharpest possible edge, because all its light comes from just one spot.)
