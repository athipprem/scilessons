Quick Recall from Lesson 1 & Unit 10 🔁

🔁 Remember the four-stage cycle? Evaporation → condensation → precipitation → collection, then it all repeats. Today we zoom in on precipitation and collection, and add one brand-new idea.
🔁 Remember Unit 10's gravity rule? "GRAVITY pulls every object toward the Earth." That's true for a dropped pencil — but did you know the MOON also has its own gravity, and it's strong enough to pull on something as big as an ocean?

Why learn this? 🤔

Knowing WHICH kind of precipitation will fall helps farmers, pilots, and weather forecasters plan ahead — snow closes roads that rain doesn't, and hail can damage crops and cars. Tides matter too: fishing boats and ferries time their trips around them, and many sea creatures and plants can only survive by adapting to water that rises and falls twice a day.
Real life: harbours build their docks to handle water levels that rise and fall every single day — if you've ever seen boats sitting on wet mud at "low tide" and floating a few hours later at "high tide," you've seen the Moon's gravity at work.

Four Kinds of Precipitation ❄️

The TEMPERATURE of the air as water falls decides which kind of precipitation you get.
🌧️
Rain
liquid water falling — happens when the air is ABOVE freezing (0°C) all the way down
❄️
Snow
soft ice crystals falling — happens when the air stays AT or BELOW freezing all the way down
🧊
Hail
hard balls of ice, formed when strong winds inside a storm cloud carry droplets up and down repeatedly, adding icy layers each time
🌨️
Sleet
snow that partly melts on the way down, then refreezes into small ice pellets before landing
Key idea: all four are made of the exact same thing — water — the only difference is the temperature it passes through as it falls, which decides its final form (the same heating/cooling idea from Units 2 and 5).

Where Collected Water Goes 🏞️

Once precipitation falls, it has to go SOMEWHERE — collection is that final gathering stage.

Oceans hold by far the MOST water on Earth — about 97% of it. Rivers and lakes collect a much smaller share on the surface. Some water soaks straight into the ground and becomes groundwater, filling tiny gaps between soil and rock like a hidden underground sponge.

A reservoir is any large area that stores collected water for later use — some are natural (a big lake), and some are built by people, like the reservoir behind a hydroelectric dam's wall that you met in Unit 18. Either way, a reservoir is just water waiting, ready to be used or to continue its journey through the cycle.

Key idea: collection isn't the "end" of the water cycle — it's simply where water waits before evaporating all over again, which is exactly why the cycle never stops.

Tides — Gravity Pulls on the Ocean 🌊

The Moon's gravity is much weaker than Earth's, but it's still strong enough to pull on something as huge as the ocean.
The Moon's gravity pulls ocean water toward it, creating a bulge (high tide) on the side of Earth facing the Moon. The Moon's pull is a little WEAKER on the far side of Earth (it's farther away), so that far side gets "left behind" and stretches into its OWN bulge too — meaning there are always two bulges at once, on opposite sides of Earth. As Earth spins once a day, each coastline passes through both bulges, giving roughly two high tides and two low tides every day.
Key idea: tides are just gravity — the exact same force from Unit 10 that pulls a dropped pencil to the ground — except here it's the Moon pulling on an entire ocean instead of Earth pulling on one small object.

Predicting Precipitation 🌳

Is the air temperature ABOVE freezing (0°C), or AT/BELOW freezing, all the way down as the water falls?
Above freezing the whole way down
Rain (liquid)
At or below freezing the whole way down
Snow (ice crystals)
If the answer is more complicated than a simple above/below split, ask: did the falling ice partly melt then refreeze ONCE on the way down, or get tossed up/down repeatedly inside the storm cloud itself?
Partly melted, then refroze once on the way down
Sleet (small ice pellets)
Tossed up/down repeatedly inside the storm cloud, building layers
Hail (layered ice balls)
How to use this: start with the simple above/below-freezing check for rain vs snow — sleet and hail are special cases where the water's temperature journey is more complicated on the way down.
