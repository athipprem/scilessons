Two Maps for Today 🗺️

MAP 1 — Heat always flows from HOT to COLD. Conduction = heat moving through touching solid matter.
MAP 2 — Scientist's tool: to compare materials fairly, change ONLY the material — keep size, water temperature, and timing the same.
🎯 By the end you can: say which materials conduct heat fast or slow, and judge whether a heat experiment is a fair test. This is exactly the kind of question ASMOPSS asks!

Why learn this? 🤔

Ever notice a metal spoon left in hot soup gets too hot to hold, but a wooden spoon stays cool? That's not luck — it's conduction, and once you understand it you can explain why pot handles are plastic, why wool keeps you warm, and why ice packs are wrapped in cloth.
Real life: chefs choose metal pans (heat spreads fast, food cooks evenly) but wooden or plastic handles (so your hand doesn't burn). Builders pack houses with insulation (poor conductors) to keep heat from escaping in winter. Your superpower: predict which material to pick for any job — fast heat transfer, or slow.

Heat & Conduction 🌡️

Heat always flows from HOT places to COLD places — never the other way round.
Touch a hot object with a cold one — which way does heat move?
↓
HOT → COLD
always this way
COLD → HOT
never happens
Conduction is heat moving through touching solid matter — like a metal spoon in hot soup. The hot end passes energy along, particle by particle, until the whole spoon warms up.

Fast Conductors vs Slow Insulators 🔥🧊

METALS (copper, iron, aluminium, steel) conduct heat FAST. Wood, plastic, rubber, wool, and air conduct heat SLOWLY — they are called INSULATORS.
GOOD CONDUCTORS (fast)
copper, aluminium, iron, steel — all metals
INSULATORS (slow)
wood, plastic, rubber, wool, air
Why it matters: we pick good conductors when we WANT heat to move fast (pots, pans), and insulators when we want to STOP heat moving (handles, oven mitts, winter coats).

Think Like a Scientist 🔬

FAIR TEST RULE: change ONE thing, measure ONE thing, keep everything else the SAME.

Imagine testing: "Which metal conducts heat fastest — copper, iron, or aluminium?" A classic way to test it: stick a small blob of wax near the top of each rod, then dip the bottom of all three rods into the SAME cup of hot water at the SAME time.

Whichever rod's wax melts FIRST is telling you: heat travelled along that rod the fastest, so that metal is the best conductor.

CHANGE
one thing
(the metal type)
MEASURE
one thing
(which wax melts first)
SAME
everything else
(rod length & thickness, wax amount, water temperature, start time)
🧠 Why "SAME" matters so much: if one rod were thicker or longer than the others, it might melt its wax slower just because of its SIZE — not because the metal itself conducts worse. That would ruin the fair test and give a wrong answer.
