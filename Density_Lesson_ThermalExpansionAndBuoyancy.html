Quick Recall from Lesson 1 🔁

🔁 Remember the density formula? Density = Mass ÷ Volume. If the MASS of something stays exactly the same but its VOLUME gets bigger, the density number gets SMALLER — the same amount of "stuff" is now spread across more space.

Today you'll see exactly when that happens in real life: whenever something is heated up.

Why learn this? 🤔

This one idea — hot stuff is less dense than cold stuff of the same substance — is why hot air balloons float upward, why the top of a swimming pool often feels warmer than the bottom, why radiators heat a whole room instead of just the air right next to them, and why weather forecasters track rising warm air to predict clouds and storms.
Real life: hot air balloon pilots control a burner to heat the air inside the balloon whenever they want to rise, and engineers leave small gaps in railway tracks and bridges specifically so the metal has room to expand on hot days without buckling.

What Heat Does to Matter 🌡️

THERMAL EXPANSION: when a substance is heated, its tiny particles move around faster and push further apart from each other — so the substance takes up MORE space (a bigger volume).
Both containers hold the exact same 9 particles (the same mass) — but the heated container is bigger, because the particles have spread further apart. Nothing was added or removed, only the space it fills changed.
Key idea: heating does NOT add any new mass, and cooling does NOT remove any mass — the exact same amount of matter is still there. Only the VOLUME changes, because the particles themselves are moving more and spreading out (when hot) or staying close together (when cold).

Why Heating Something Lowers Its Density ⚖️

Density = Mass ÷ Volume. Heating keeps the MASS the same but INCREASES the volume — so the density number goes DOWN.
🌡️⬆️
Heating
same mass, bigger volume → LOWER density (less tightly packed)
🌡️⬇️
Cooling
same mass, smaller volume → HIGHER density (more tightly packed)
Key idea: this works for solids, liquids, and gases — hot air is less dense than cold air, hot water is less dense than cold water, and even hot metal is (very slightly) less dense than cold metal of the same piece.

Buoyancy: Warm Rises Through Cool 🎈

Remember Unit 15's float/sink rule: less dense floats, more dense sinks. Since warm fluid is LESS dense than cool fluid of the SAME substance, warm fluid rises up through the cooler fluid around it.
The air inside a hot air balloon is heated, making it less dense than the cooler air outside. Since it's less dense than its surroundings, it rises — exactly like a cork floating up through water, just with air instead of water.
Everyday example — a pot of water on a stove: the water at the bottom heats up first, becomes less dense, and rises to the top. Cooler, denser water from the top then sinks down to take its place. This constant swapping is called a convection current — it's also exactly how warm air rises in a room and how weather systems move heat around the planet.
Bonus puzzle — why does a heavy steel ship float, when a solid lump of steel sinks? Steel itself IS denser than water — a solid steel block sinks instantly. But a ship isn't solid steel — it's a hollow SHAPE, with a huge amount of empty air space inside its hull. That trapped air spreads the ship's total mass out over a much bigger overall volume, so the ship's OVERALL density (all its steel PLUS all that trapped air, combined) ends up LESS than water's density — even though the steel it's made from is denser on its own. This is the exact same density-vs-liquid rule from Unit 15, just applied to the ship's overall shape instead of to the raw material alone.

Will It Rise or Sink? 🌳

Comparing two patches of the SAME substance — is one WARMER than the other?
The warmer patch
Less dense → RISES through the cooler patch
The cooler patch
More dense → SINKS below the warmer patch
How to use this: this only works when comparing the SAME substance at two different temperatures (warm air vs cool air, warm water vs cool water) — it's really just Unit 15's float/sink rule, applied to temperature differences instead of two totally different substances.
