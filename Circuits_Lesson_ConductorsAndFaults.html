Two Maps for Today 🗺️

MAP 1 — Materials are either ELECTRICAL CONDUCTORS (let electricity flow through) or INSULATORS (block it).
MAP 2 — Scientist's tool: use a step-by-step check to find WHY a circuit isn't working, and design a fair test to compare materials.
🎯 By the end you can: sort materials into electrical conductors and insulators, work through a fault-finding check when a bulb won't light, and judge a fair test comparing which material conducts electricity.

Why learn this? 🤔

Wires are covered in plastic for a reason — the metal inside conducts electricity to where it's needed, while the plastic coating insulates YOU from it, keeping you safe. Electricians, engineers, and appliance designers rely on knowing exactly which materials conduct and which don't.
Real life: a metal fork left touching a toaster's inner wires could conduct electricity dangerously — that's why appliances use plastic and rubber parts anywhere you might touch them. Choosing the wrong material in the wrong place isn't just a bad circuit, it can be a safety hazard.

Electrical Conductors vs Insulators 🔌

A CONDUCTOR lets electricity flow through it easily. An INSULATOR blocks electricity from flowing.
⚡ Good electrical conductors
metals: copper wire, iron, steel, aluminium
🚫 Good electrical insulators
plastic, rubber, glass, dry wood, paper
Recall from Unit 3: metals were also GOOD HEAT conductors, while wood and plastic were POOR heat conductors (good insulators). It's the same materials behaving the same way — metals conduct both heat AND electricity; wood and plastic block both.
Careful: "conductor" always needs to say WHAT it conducts. A material could be a good heat conductor but you should still check separately whether it's a good electrical conductor too — for most everyday materials (metal vs wood/plastic) they match, but don't just assume without thinking it through.

Diagnosing Circuit Faults 🔍

When a bulb won't light, check each part of the loop in order — battery, wires, switch, bulb — until you find the gap.
Bulb won't light. Is the battery fresh and facing the correct way?
↓ if yes
Are all the wire connections tight, with no loose ends?
↓ if yes
Is the switch CLOSED (not left open)?
↓ if yes
Is the bulb's filament unbroken (not "burnt out")?
↓
If EVERY check passes, the circuit should be complete — the bulb lights up 💡
The FIRST check that fails is the fault — fix that one thing, then re-test
Key idea: this is exactly a fault-finding checklist — go through the possible gaps ONE AT A TIME in order, rather than guessing randomly. It's the same "check one thing at a time" habit you used for reading a two-property classification chart.

Think Like a Scientist 🔬

FAIR TEST RULE: change ONE thing (the material), measure ONE thing, keep everything else the SAME.

Imagine testing: "Which of four objects — a metal paperclip, a plastic ruler, a rubber band, and a wooden pencil — conducts electricity?" Build a simple circuit with a gap, then place each object in the gap one at a time to see if the bulb lights.

CHANGE
one thing
(the object placed in the gap)
MEASURE
one thing
(does the bulb light up: yes/no)
SAME
everything else
(same battery, same wires, same bulb, same gap size)
🧠 Why "SAME" matters: if you swapped to a weaker battery halfway through testing, an object might seem "non-conducting" just because the battery was weaker — not because of the material itself. That would ruin the fair comparison.
Predicting the result: based on what you know about conductors and insulators, which of the four objects should let the bulb light up? (The metal paperclip — it's the only conductor of the four; the ruler, rubber band, and pencil are all insulators.)

Your Turn — Check Yourself ✅

Answer first, then press Check.

1. Which of these is the BEST electrical conductor?

✔ Right — copper is a metal, and metals are good electrical conductors.
✘ Look for the metal in the list — metals conduct electricity well; rubber, wood, and plastic are insulators.

2. A bulb won't light. The battery is fresh and facing the right way, and the switch is closed. What should be checked NEXT, before assuming the bulb itself is broken?

✔ Correct — work through the checklist in order: battery, then connections, then switch, then bulb.
✘ Don't jump straight to "the bulb is broken" — check the wire connections first, since a loose wire is a very common fault.

3. To fairly test which of four objects conducts electricity, what MUST stay exactly the same across all four tests?

✔ Correct — everything except the object itself must stay identical for a fair comparison.
✘ A fair test changes only ONE thing (the object) — the battery, wires, bulb, and gap size must all stay the same.

4. A wire is covered in a plastic coating. What is the MAIN reason plastic is used for the coating, not another metal?

✔ Exactly — the plastic coating is an insulator, keeping the electricity inside the wire and people safely outside it.
✘ Think about what an insulator does — it BLOCKS electricity, which is exactly what you want between a live wire and someone's hand.
