Quick Recall from Unit 3 🧲

🔁 Remember magnetism? You couldn't SEE the magnetic force pulling two magnets together — but it was completely real, and you learned rules to predict it.

Microorganisms are the same kind of idea: invisible to your eyes, but very real and very powerful. A single microorganism is far too small to see, but billions of them together can turn milk into yogurt, make bread rise, or make old food unsafe to eat.

Mini self-check: in Unit 3, could you see the magnetic pull with your eyes? (Answer: no — but you could still test and prove it was there. Keep that "invisible but provable" idea in mind for microorganisms.)

Why learn this? 🤔

Every time bread rises, yogurt sets, or leftovers get thrown out because they've "gone off," microorganisms are at work. Knowing how they behave helps you understand food safety, cooking, farming, and how nature recycles dead plants and animals. ASMOPSS loves asking about mould/bacteria experiments — exactly the kind of question you'll master today and next lesson.
Real life: bakers use a fungus called yeast to make bread dough rise. Farmers rely on bacteria and fungi in soil to break down dead leaves into nutrients their crops can use. Doctors and parents care about handwashing because it removes harmful bacteria and viruses before they can make us sick.

What Are Microorganisms? 🔬

Microorganisms are LIVING THINGS so tiny you need a microscope to see them. The main groups are BACTERIA and FUNGI (like mould and yeast).
🦠
Bacteria
tiny single-celled living things — found almost everywhere: soil, water, food, even your own body
🍄
Fungi
mould and yeast are both fungi — mould grows fuzzy patches on old food; yeast makes bread rise
🔎
Too small to see
a single one is invisible — you only notice them once there are millions together
Key fact: microorganisms are alive — they grow, use food and energy, and reproduce (make more of themselves), just like bigger living things. They just do it at a size you can't see with your eyes alone.

Helpful or Harmful? Use This Rule 🔄

The SAME kinds of microorganisms can be helpful in one situation and harmful in another — it depends on WHERE they are growing and WHAT they are doing there.
Is the microorganism doing a job we WANT (making food, recycling waste) or damaging something we need (spoiling food, making us sick)?
↓
Doing a job we want
HELPFUL
Damaging food/health

✅
HELPFUL examples
yeast rising bread, bacteria turning milk into yogurt, soil bacteria/fungi breaking down dead leaves into nutrients
⚠️
HARMFUL examples
mould growing on old bread, bacteria spoiling forgotten leftovers, germs that spread coughs and colds
🔑 The test: "is this microorganism making something useful, or ruining something we need?" That tells you helpful or harmful — not the microorganism's name alone.

Decomposers: Nature's Recyclers ♻️

Decomposers are microorganisms (mostly bacteria and fungi) that break down dead plants, dead animals, and food waste — and recycle the nutrients back into the soil.
Why this matters for nature: without decomposers, dead leaves, fallen trees, and dead animals would just pile up forever, and the nutrients trapped inside them could never be reused. Decomposers unlock those nutrients and put them back into the soil so new plants can grow — that's why they are HELPFUL, even though they are technically "rotting" something.
Compost bins at home or school work the exact same way — you're giving decomposer microorganisms food scraps and garden waste so they can turn it into nutrient-rich soil.
