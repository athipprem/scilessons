Quick Recall from Lesson 1 🔁

🔁 Remember Lesson 1? Work = Force × Distance moved — and if nothing moves, the work done is exactly ZERO, no matter how much force is involved. Simple machines (levers, ramps, pulleys) trade force for distance, never creating energy for free.

Today you'll turn Unit 18's "height + gravity = stored energy" idea into an actual number you can calculate — and extend Unit 10's tug-of-war idea to figure out what happens when two forces act on something at the same time.

Why learn this? 🤔

Engineers calculate exact numbers like these every single day — a crane operator needs to know exactly how much lifting force is required before hoisting a steel beam, and a helicopter pilot needs to know the engine must supply enough force to balance the aircraft's weight, or it will fall. Being able to calculate energy and force — not just describe them in words — is exactly the kind of applied science ASMOPSS rewards at the highest level.
Real life: a tug-of-war team calculates whether they're winning by comparing total pulling force on each side — the same combining-forces idea you'll use today.

Calculating Stored Energy & Lifting Force ⛰️

Mechanical (potential) energy = mass × 10 × height. Lifting force needed to hold something still = mass × 10.
Where does the "10" come from? Gravity pulls every kilogram down with a certain strength. Scientists round that pulling strength to about 10 for every kilogram, to keep the maths simple — it's the same gravity idea from Unit 18 and Unit 21, just turned into a number you can multiply with.
Worked example — a 2 kg toy drone hovering 5 m above the ground:
Mechanical (potential) energy = mass × 10 × height = 2 × 10 × 5 = 100 J (Joules — the unit for energy)
Lifting force needed to hover = mass × 10 = 2 × 10 = 20 N (Newtons — the unit for force)
Work done WHILE hovering perfectly still = 0 J — because the drone isn't actually moving, just like Lesson 1's "pushing the wall" example!
Key idea: the drone's ENGINE still has to keep pushing with 20 N of force every second just to stay up (fighting gravity) — but because the drone itself doesn't move up or down while hovering, no scientific WORK is being done on it, even though the motor is working hard.

Combining Forces: Add or Cancel? 🤼

Forces pulling or pushing in the SAME direction ADD UP fully. Forces pulling or pushing in OPPOSITE directions SUBTRACT (partly or fully cancel).
Left: two friends push the SAME box in the SAME direction with 10N and 15N — the box feels a full 25N push. Right: two dogs pull a rope in OPPOSITE directions with 20N and 12N — the pulls partly cancel, leaving a net 8N pull toward the stronger dog's side.
This is Unit 10's tug-of-war rule, extended: when both sides pull EQUALLY hard in opposite directions, the forces cancel completely (balanced, no movement — Unit 10's rope that "isn't moving"). When one side pulls harder, the forces don't fully cancel — the leftover (the stronger side's force minus the weaker side's force) is what actually moves the object, toward the stronger side.

Adding or Cancelling? 🌳

Are the two forces pointing in the SAME direction, or OPPOSITE directions?
SAME direction
ADD them fully together
OPPOSITE directions, EQUAL size
CANCEL completely — balanced, no movement
OPPOSITE directions, UNEQUAL size
SUBTRACT — net force toward the stronger side
How to use this: always check direction FIRST, before doing any adding or subtracting. Same direction never cancels; opposite directions never simply add.
