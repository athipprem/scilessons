Why learn this? 🤔

A plant has no mouth and no heart — yet water travels from the soil all the way up to the highest leaf. How? Once you know, you can predict and even test what a plant will do.
Real life: ever put a white flower (or celery) in coloured water and watched it turn colour? 🌷➡️💙 Florists use this trick. Farmers need it too — if roots can't move water up, the plant wilts. Your superpower: explain and design these experiments like a real scientist.

Step 1 — Roots Drink 🥕

Water enters through tiny ROOT HAIRS, like hundreds of straws.
What happens: water (with minerals) from the soil soaks into the root hairs. Now it must travel UP to the rest of the plant.

Step 2 — The Stem Lifts Water Up 🪵

Inside the stem are tiny TUBES that carry water up to every leaf and flower.
The classic experiment: put a white flower in blue-coloured water. A few hours later the petals turn blue! That proves the water travelled up the stem all the way to the flower.

Step 3 — Leaves Release Water 🍃

Leaves have tiny HOLES. Water leaves as vapour — and that pulls more water up.
Going out: water escapes from tiny holes in the leaves as invisible vapour. As water leaves the top, it pulls more water up from the roots — like sipping a straw.
The leaf-bubble experiment: put a leaf in warm water and you'll see tiny bubbles escape — that's air coming out of those tiny holes and air spaces inside the leaf. It shows leaves are full of little air passages.

Think Like a Scientist 🔬

FAIR TEST RULE: change ONE thing, measure ONE thing, keep everything else the SAME.

Imagine testing: "Does light make a plant's leaves greener?" To be fair:

CHANGE
one thing
(amount of light)
MEASURE
one thing
(how green)
SAME
everything else
(water, pot, plant type)
Why a "same" plant matters (the control): if you only test the plant in light, you can't be sure light did it. You need a matching plant kept in the dark to compare. That second set-up is the control. To prove water reached the flower, the set-up must actually have a flower and compare fairly — that's what those experiment questions check!
🧠 Big habit: in any experiment question, ask "What changed? What stayed the same? What are they comparing?"
