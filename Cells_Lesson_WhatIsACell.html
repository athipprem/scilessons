Quick Fix from Unit 13 💡

Before we start Cells, let's lock in one idea from Light that tripped up a few questions last time.

The rule, both directions:

A POINT source (like a distant star, or a tiny bulb far away) sends light from just ONE place. Every spot behind an object is either fully lit or fully blocked — so the shadow has ONE sharp, crisp edge, with no fuzzy fringe at all.

A BROAD source (like a long fluorescent tube, or a big light close up) sends light from MANY different places at once. Right behind the object is still fully dark — but just to the sides, light from SOME parts of the source sneaks past while light from other parts is still blocked. That fuzzy, partly-lit sliver is the penumbra.

So: POINT source → sharp shadow, no penumbra. BROAD source → soft shadow, with a penumbra fringe. It's easy to accidentally flip these two around — let's check both directions before moving on.

Quick Check 1: A single tiny LED bulb, far across a dark room, shines on a toy. What should its shadow's edge look like?

✔ Right — a single point source always gives a sharp edge, since light only comes from one place.
✘ Remember: a point source sends light from just ONE place — what kind of edge does that give?

Quick Check 2: Two lamps are compared — a small bare bulb vs a large flat panel light, same distance away. Which one gives the SOFTER, fuzzier shadow edge?

✔ Correct — the larger, broader light source is the one that gives the softer, fuzzier edge.
✘ Which lamp sends light from MANY different places at once — the small bulb, or the large panel?

Quick Check 3: A shadow has a dark centre with a soft, blurry fringe around it. What KIND of light source most likely made it?

✔ Exactly — that soft, blurry fringe (the penumbra) is the signature of a broad source, not a point source.
✘ A soft, blurry fringe around a shadow only happens when light comes from many places — point or broad?

Quick Recall from Unit 8 🔁

🔁 Photosynthesis check: back in Unit 8, you learned that chlorophyll — the green pigment that captures sunlight for photosynthesis — lives inside tiny structures called chloroplasts.

Here's the connection: chloroplasts are actually one of the special parts found ONLY inside plant cells, not animal cells. Today you'll meet the whole cell and see exactly where chloroplasts fit in.

Why learn this? 🤔

Understanding cells explains why plants can make their own food but animals can't, why plant stems and trunks can stand up rigid and tall while animal bodies stay soft and flexible, and how doctors and scientists study diseases that start when something goes wrong inside a cell.
Real life: scientists use powerful microscopes to study cells directly, farmers rely on understanding plant cells to grow healthier crops, and doctors study human cells to understand and treat illness — cells are the starting point for almost all of biology and medicine.

Meet the Cell Parts 🔬

Every cell — plant or animal — has these 3 parts in common: nucleus, cytoplasm, and cell membrane.
🧬 Found in BOTH plant and animal cells
  • Nucleus — the control centre, holds instructions
  • Cytoplasm — the jelly-like filling
  • Cell membrane — thin, flexible boundary
🌿 Found ONLY in plant cells
  • Cell wall — rigid outer layer, for support
  • Chloroplasts — capture sunlight for photosynthesis
  • Large permanent vacuole — stores water, keeps the cell firm
Key idea: the plant-only parts are exactly what let plants make their own food, stand up straight without a skeleton, and stay firm with stored water — jobs animal cells simply don't need to do the same way.

What Do They Actually Look Like? 👀

The cell wall is exactly why plant cells look like neat rectangular boxes, while animal cells look round and a little irregular.
Plant cells look like neat, rectangular boxes because of the thick, rigid cell wall around the outside. Inside: a large vacuole, a nucleus, and several green chloroplasts.
Animal cells look round and a bit irregular because they only have a thin, flexible cell membrane — no rigid wall. There's no cell wall, no chloroplasts, and no large permanent vacuole.
Key idea: if you see a boxy, rectangular cell with a thick outer line, chloroplasts, and a big vacuole — it's a plant cell. If you see a round, irregular cell with none of those — it's an animal cell.

Plant Cell or Animal Cell? 🔍

Ask this one key question about any cell you see under a microscope.
Does this cell have a thick, rigid outer wall AND green chloroplasts inside it?
YES
It's a PLANT cell — the wall and chloroplasts are found only in plants.
NO
It's an ANIMAL cell — just a flexible membrane, no wall, no chloroplasts.
Key idea: the cell wall and chloroplasts are the two dead giveaways for a plant cell — if either is missing, you're looking at an animal cell.
