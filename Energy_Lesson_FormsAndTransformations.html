Quick Recall from Unit 6 & Unit 15 🔁

🔁 Remember circuits? Back in Unit 6 you learned that a working circuit sends energy through a chain: chemical → electrical → light/heat (say, a battery lighting a bulb). That's actually your very first example of an energy transformation chain!

Today you'll meet several MORE forms of energy beyond chemical, electrical, light, and heat — and learn a simple, general rule for describing any energy chain, not just circuits.

🔁 Remember Unit 15's Observation vs Inference rule? An OBSERVATION is something you can directly see, hear, or measure. An INFERENCE is a conclusion you WORK OUT based on that observation. This is a "Think Like a Scientist" skill that comes up in EVERY unit's harder questions, not just density — so let's practise it again with two fresh examples before today's energy content.
Refresher Q1: A student watches a toy car roll down a ramp and speed up as it goes. She writes: "The car's kinetic energy must be increasing as it speeds up." Is this the OBSERVATION or the INFERENCE?

Answer: this is the INFERENCE — she didn't directly see "kinetic energy," she worked out that idea from what she actually saw (the car speeding up).
Refresher Q2: A student holds a stretched rubber band and lets it go, watching it snap forward across the table. What she directly SAW was: "the rubber band snapped forward and slid across the table." What she WORKED OUT afterwards was: "the stretched rubber band must have been storing energy." Notice the pattern — the observation is always the plain, directly-seen event; the inference is always the explanation or conclusion drawn from it.

Why learn this? 🤔

Every machine, meal, and power plant on Earth works by transforming energy from one form into another. Understanding these chains helps you see why a torch needs batteries, why exercise makes you warm, and why power plants (including hydroelectric dams) can turn falling water into the electricity in your home.
Real life: when you eat food, your body transforms the chemical energy stored in it into kinetic energy (to move), thermal energy (to keep you warm), and more — the exact same "one form becomes another" idea you'll use for every machine and natural process today.

The Main Forms of Energy 🔋

Energy always exists in a FORM — here are the ones you'll use most often.
🏃
Kinetic
the energy of anything moving
⛰️
Potential
stored energy, ready to be released (height or stretching)
🍞
Chemical
stored in food, fuel, and batteries
🔥
Thermal (heat)
the energy of warmth
💡
Light
energy that lets you see
🔊
Sound
energy carried by vibrations
Key idea: "electrical energy" is really just energy flowing through wires — it's useful because it converts easily into almost any other form (light, heat, sound, motion), which is why it powers so many machines.

Energy Transformation Chains 🔗

A TRANSFORMATION CHAIN shows energy changing from one form into another, one step at a time.

Example 1 — a torch:

🔋 Chemical (battery)
→
⚡ Electrical (wire)
→
💡 Light (bulb)

Example 2 — dropping a ball:

⛰️ Potential (held up high)
→
🏃 Kinetic (falling)

Example 3 — rubbing your hands together:

🏃 Kinetic (rubbing motion)
→
🔥 Thermal (your hands feel warm)
Key idea: a chain can have TWO steps or more — energy keeps transforming until it usually ends up spread out as heat and sound, which is why machines eventually need more fuel or a fresh battery.

Energy Is Never Lost — Only Changed ♻️

THE BIG RULE: energy cannot be created out of nothing, and it cannot simply disappear — it can only be transformed from one form into another.
Surprise: when a bouncing ball eventually stops bouncing, its energy hasn't vanished — it has transformed into thermal energy (the ball and floor warm up very slightly) and sound energy (the noise of each bounce), spread out so much you can't easily notice it anymore.
🔎 This is why "wasted energy" really means "energy that transformed into a form we didn't want" — like a lightbulb that gets hot (wasted thermal energy) as well as making light (the form we wanted).

Which Transformation Is This? 🌳

Look at the SITUATION: what form of energy do you START with, and what form do you END with?
Something is STORED (height, stretch, or fuel) and starts to MOVE or RELEASE
Potential (or chemical) → Kinetic (or another form)
Something is ALREADY MOVING and slows down, heats up, or makes noise
Kinetic → Thermal (and/or Sound)
How to use this: always ask "what form did it have BEFORE?" and "what form does it have AFTER?" — naming both ends of the chain is the key skill, not memorising every possible example.
