Quick Recall from Lesson 1 & Unit 17 🔁

🔁 Remember the flower parts and the cycle? A flower has parts that make pollen and parts that receive pollen and make seeds. Pollen travels from an ANTHER to a STIGMA (pollination), then down the STYLE to join an OVULE inside the OVARY (fertilization) — and the ovule becomes a new seed.

Today you'll group those parts into two clear teams so anther and ovary never get muddled again, then find out what happens to the seed AFTER it forms.

Why learn this? 🤔

Plant breeders control exactly which flower's pollen reaches which flower's stigma to create new crop varieties — which means knowing precisely which parts are male and which are female. And every dandelion clock, burr stuck to a sock, or coconut washed up on a beach is a real example of a seed's journey away from its parent plant.
Real life: farmers sometimes use a small paintbrush to move pollen from one flower's anther to another flower's stigma by hand, when they want to control exactly which two plants combine to make seeds.

Two Teams: Male Parts vs Female Parts ♂️♀️

Every flower part belongs to one of two teams: the STAMEN (male, makes pollen) or the PISTIL (female, receives pollen and makes seeds).
The stamen (orange, left) is the anther sitting atop a thin filament. The pistil (purple, right) is the sticky stigma leading down a style into the swollen ovary at the base.
♂️ STAMEN — male parts
Anther: sits on top, makes the powdery pollen.
Filament: the thin stalk that holds the anther up.
♀️ PISTIL — female parts
Stigma: the sticky top that catches pollen.
Style: the tube pollen travels down.
Ovary: holds the ovule(s); becomes the fruit/seed container.
Memory trick: "Anther" and "stamen" both start with a sound near the front of your mouth, like the word "add" — think of the anther ADDING pollen. "Ovary" sounds like "oven" — think of the ovary as the little oven where the seed actually bakes and grows, tucked at the BASE of the flower, not the top. If a question asks which part MAKES pollen, the answer is always on the male (stamen) team — anther. If it asks which part BECOMES the seed/fruit, the answer is always on the female (pistil) team — ovary.

The Full Cycle, Start to Finish 🐝

🐝 Pollen leaves the anther (male)
→
🌸 Lands on a stigma (female) — POLLINATION
→
⬇️ Travels down the style
→
🥚 Joins an ovule in the ovary — FERTILIZATION
→
🌰 Ovule becomes a NEW SEED
Key idea: pollination (landing on the stigma) always happens before fertilization (joining an ovule) — and pollination alone doesn't guarantee fertilization will follow. Once fertilized, the ovary often swells into a fruit, protecting the new seeds inside.

The Missing Piece: How Do Seeds Travel? 🌬️

A seed that just falls straight down would have to compete with its own parent plant for light, water, and space — so plants have evolved clever ways to send seeds further away, called SEED DISPERSAL.
Three real dispersal methods: a fluffy dandelion seed drifting on the wind, a spiky burr seed hitching a ride stuck to an animal's fur, and a coconut floating on ocean currents.
🌬️
Wind
light, feathery, or wing-shaped seeds (dandelion fluff, maple "helicopter" wings) get carried on the breeze
🐿️
Animals
spiky/hooked seeds (burrs) stick to fur; other seeds are eaten inside fruit and pass through droppings far away
🌊
Water
tough, buoyant seeds (coconuts) float on rivers or oceans until they wash ashore somewhere new
Key idea: a seed's physical features are a clue to its dispersal method — feathery or winged shapes suggest wind, hooks or spikes suggest animal fur, and a hard waterproof shell that floats suggests water.

Match the Seed to Its Dispersal Method 🌳

Look closely at the seed's own physical features first.
