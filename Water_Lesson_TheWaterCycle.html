Quick Fix from Unit 18 💡

Before we start the Water Cycle, let's lock in one idea from Energy that tripped up several questions last time.

The rule, both directions:

KINETIC energy is the energy of anything that is ACTUALLY MOVING right now — a spinning fan blade, blowing wind, a rolling ball, water rushing downhill. If it's moving, it's kinetic.

POTENTIAL energy is STORED energy an object has because of its POSITION — usually because it's high up, or stretched/squeezed — even though right now it might be completely STILL. A book on a high shelf, a stretched bow, water sitting still in a high-up reservoir — none of these are moving, but they're storing energy ready to be released.

So: MOVING → KINETIC. STILL BUT HIGH UP OR STRETCHED → POTENTIAL. It's easy to mix these two up — especially inside a chain where something starts still and ends up moving, or when you're tracking energy at several different points along one path. Let's check all three ways this can trip you up.

Quick Check 1: A water wheel spins around and around as a stream flows past and pushes on it. What kind of energy does the SPINNING wheel have?

✔ Right — the wheel is actually MOVING, so its energy is kinetic, not potential.
✘ Remember: if something is actually moving right now, that's kinetic energy — not potential.

Quick Check 2: A roller coaster cart is pulled slowly to the very top of a hill, then pauses for a second before racing down. At that exact moment — at the top, not yet moving — is its energy mostly potential or mostly kinetic?

✔ Correct — at the very top, still and not yet moving, its energy is mostly potential (stored, because of its height).
✘ At the top of the hill the cart isn't moving yet — still + high up = which kind of energy?

Quick Check 3: Which of these is the BEST definition of POTENTIAL energy?

✔ Exactly — potential energy is stored energy from height or position, not motion.
✘ Potential energy is about being STILL but STORED (height/position) — not about moving.

Quick Recall from Unit 2 & Unit 5 🔁

🔁 Remember changes of state? Back in Units 2 and 5 you learned that heating a liquid until it becomes a gas is called EVAPORATING, and cooling a gas back into a liquid is called CONDENSING. You even learned that "weather depends on evaporation and condensation — that's how clouds and rain form!"

Today you'll use those exact two words again — evaporating and condensing are two of the four stages of the water cycle, the same idea applied to the whole planet's water instead of just a puddle or a window.

Why learn this? 🤔

The water cycle is why rain falls, why rivers never run dry, and why the same water has been reused on Earth for billions of years — not one drop is ever truly lost. Farmers plan their crops around it, weather forecasters predict it, and every glass of water you drink has been through this cycle more times than you can count.
Real life: the water in a rain puddle today might have been part of the ocean last month, floated as a cloud last week, and could end up as snow on a mountain next year — the same water, just changing form and location over and over again.

The Four Stages of the Water Cycle 🔄

The water cycle has FOUR main stages that repeat forever, in the same order.
☀️
1. Evaporation
the Sun heats water in oceans, lakes & rivers, turning it into invisible water vapour that rises into the air
☁️
2. Condensation
as water vapour rises high up, it cools down and turns back into tiny liquid droplets, forming clouds
🌧️
3. Precipitation
when a cloud's droplets get big and heavy enough, they fall back down as rain, snow, or hail
🌊
4. Collection
fallen water gathers in oceans, lakes, rivers, and soaks into the ground — ready to evaporate again
Key idea: evaporation and condensation are exact opposites of each other (just like you learned with puddles and cold windows) — the water cycle is really just those two familiar changes of state happening on a planet-sized scale, plus precipitation and collection to complete the loop.

One Endless Loop ♻️

Water evaporates from the ocean (bottom) into the sky, condenses into clouds (top), falls back down as precipitation, and collects in the ocean again — then the whole loop repeats.
Key idea: this is a genuine CYCLE, not a one-way trip — there's no true "start" or "end," and the same water molecules go around this loop again and again. That's a similar idea to Unit 18's conservation-of-energy rule (energy is never created or destroyed, only changed in form) — except here it's WATER that keeps moving and changing form (liquid → vapour → liquid) without ever disappearing.

Which Stage Is This? 🌳

Is the water turning from LIQUID into VAPOUR, or from VAPOUR into LIQUID droplets/clouds?
Liquid heating up and rising as invisible vapour
Evaporation
Vapour cooling down and forming visible droplets/clouds
Condensation
Is water FALLING from the sky, or GATHERING back into oceans/rivers/ground?
Water falling as rain, snow, or hail
Precipitation
Fallen water gathering in oceans, lakes, rivers, or soil
Collection
How to use this: first ask whether the water is changing FORM (liquid ↔ vapour) or MOVING to a new PLACE (falling or gathering) — that narrows it to two stages, then the direction tells you exactly which one.
