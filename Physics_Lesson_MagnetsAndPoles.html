Quick Recall from Unit 2 🎈

🔁 Remember gases? Air is invisible, but it's still real matter with real mass — you proved it with the balloon and the football. You can't SEE it, but you can prove it's there.

Magnetism works the same way: you can't see the magnetic pull, but it's a real force you can prove and predict — just like you proved invisible air has mass. Today you'll learn the exact rules that control that invisible pull.

Mini self-check: in Unit 2, did a full balloon weigh (1) the same, or (2) more than an empty one? (Answer: more — the invisible gas inside still has mass. Keep that "invisible but real" idea in mind for magnetism.)

Why learn this? 🤔

Compasses, fridge magnets, the click of a magnetic door catch, machines that sort metal from rubbish at recycling plants — all of these rely on the SAME two rules you're about to learn. ASMOPSS loves asking "will these magnets attract or repel?" questions.
Real life: a compass needle is a tiny magnet that always points North because it lines up with Earth's own huge magnetic field. Recycling machines use giant magnets to pull out steel cans but leave aluminium cans behind — because not all metals are magnetic!

Which Materials Are Magnetic? 🔍

A magnet only pulls on a small group of metals — mainly IRON, STEEL, NICKEL and COBALT.

Common trap: people think ALL metals are magnetic. They are not!

🧲
IS pulled by a magnet
iron nail, steel paperclip, steel spoon, a tin (steel) can
🚫
NOT pulled by a magnet
aluminium can, copper wire, wood, plastic, glass, paper, gold, most coins
🔑 The test: "is it made of iron or steel?" If yes → magnetic. If it's a different metal (aluminium, copper, gold) or not a metal at all → not magnetic.

Every Magnet Has Two Poles 🔴🔵

The two ends of a magnet are called the NORTH pole and the SOUTH pole. The pull is strongest right at the poles.
Key fact: you cannot have a magnet with only ONE pole. Even if you cut a bar magnet in half, each new piece grows its own North AND South pole.

Attract or Repel? Use This Rule 🔄

SAME poles facing each other → REPEL (push apart). DIFFERENT poles facing each other → ATTRACT (pull together).
Are the two facing poles the SAME? (N–N or S–S)
↓
YES → REPEL
push apart
NO (N–S) → ATTRACT
pull together

Reading a Row of Magnets 🧲🧲🧲

Some ASMOPSS questions show several magnets touching in a line and ask which arrangement is POSSIBLE. Use the same rule, joint by joint.
Every touching join here is S touching N → ATTRACT → the row can stay together ✓
How to check any row: look at EACH touching point on its own. If that one join is two different poles (N–S), it holds together. If even ONE join is two matching poles (N–N or S–S), the magnets push apart right there — the whole row can't stay lined up like that.
Try it in your head: in the row above, every join is S touching N, so the whole chain attracts and stays together. If we flipped just the middle magnet around, its left end would become S facing the S next to it — that ONE join would repel, and the row would break apart there.

Your Turn — Check Yourself ✅

Pick an answer, then press Check. No peeking — try first!

1. Which of these objects would a magnet NOT pull on?

✔ Right! Plastic is not a magnetic material — magnets only pull iron, steel, nickel and cobalt.
✘ Ask: is it made of iron or steel? Plastic is neither.

2. Two magnets are held with their South poles facing each other. What happens?

✔ Correct — matching poles (S–S) always repel.
✘ Remember the rule: SAME poles facing each other push apart.

3. An aluminium drink can is placed near a strong magnet. What happens?

✔ Yes — aluminium is a metal, but it is NOT one of the magnetic metals (iron, steel, nickel, cobalt).
✘ Careful — not all metals are magnetic. Aluminium is the classic exception.

4. Two bar magnets are placed end to end, touching. The join is North touching South. What will happen?

✔ Exactly — N touching S is two DIFFERENT poles, so they attract.
✘ Different poles (N and S) facing each other always attract.
