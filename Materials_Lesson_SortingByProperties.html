Quick Recall from Unit 3 🔁

🔁 Magnetism check: back in Unit 3, you learned that magnets attract certain materials, like steel — we call steel a MAGNETIC material.

Here's the tricky part worth re-checking: being ATTRACTED to a magnet does NOT make something a magnet itself. A steel paperclip pulled toward a magnet is just a magnetic material doing what magnetic materials do — it can't go on to pull other paperclips around on its own the way a real magnet can. Keeping this distinction clear matters just as much as the new properties you'll sort by today — it's easy to mix up "is attracted to X" with "is X."

Why learn this? 🤔

Engineers pick hard materials for tools and buildings, and soft materials for cushions and clothes. Packaging designers choose flexible materials so boxes and wrapping can bend without cracking. Understanding floating and sinking explains how boats stay afloat, why life jackets work, and why oil spilled on the sea floats on top of the water instead of mixing in.
Real life: a phone case is often soft and flexible (to absorb a drop), while the screen glass underneath is hard and rigid. Shipbuilders design boat hulls from materials and shapes that float. Oil-spill clean-up crews rely on the fact that oil floats on top of water to skim it off the surface.

Two More Ways to Sort 🧱

HARD/SOFT is about resisting pressure. FLEXIBLE/RIGID is about bending without breaking.
HARD
keeps its shape under pressure
e.g. metal, glass, brick
SOFT
squashes or presses in easily
e.g. sponge, wool, foam
FLEXIBLE
bends without snapping
e.g. rubber, wire, cloth
NOT FLEXIBLE (rigid)
snaps or stays straight
e.g. glass, dry wood, ceramic
Key idea: hard/soft and flexible/rigid are TWO SEPARATE properties, not the same thing — glass is hard AND rigid (snaps rather than bends), while rubber is soft-ish AND very flexible. A material's hardness doesn't automatically tell you whether it bends.

Floating & Sinking in Different Liquids 💧

Whether a material floats or sinks depends on how heavy it is for its size — and that can even change between liquids.
Cork
floats in water AND in cooking oil
A metal paperclip
sinks in water AND in cooking oil
A chunk of beeswax
floats in water, but SINKS in cooking oil
Key idea: water and cooking oil aren't equally "heavy for their size" as each other either — that's why beeswax can float in one but sink in the other. We'll dig into exactly why in a future unit on density; for now, practise reading and using given floating/sinking results, the way a real classification flowchart does.

Follow the Classification Flowchart 🔍

Real classification puzzles give you a flowchart AND some already-known results — your job is to follow the logic, not guess.
Is the material HARD or SOFT?
↓
HARD
Does it SINK or FLOAT in oil?
e.g. metal sinks in oil; plastic can float on oil
SOFT
Is it FLEXIBLE or NOT FLEXIBLE?
e.g. rubber is flexible; dry wood is not
Key idea: follow the flowchart one branch at a time — first sort hard vs soft, THEN apply the second question only to the branch the material landed on. A material never needs both the "sinks/floats" question AND the "flexible" question — only one, depending on its first answer.
