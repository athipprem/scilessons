Quick Recall from Unit 11 🔁

🔁 Remember digestion? Back in Unit 11 you learned that your digestive system breaks food down into tiny pieces so it can pass into your blood, and your circulatory system then carries those pieces to every cell in your body, where they get used.

Today's question is: once those broken-down pieces of food are in your blood, WHAT exactly are they, and what does each one actually do for your body? That's what "nutrients" means — the useful substances your body pulls out of food.

Why learn this? 🤔

Eating a balanced mix of nutrients gives you steady energy for school and sport, helps your body grow and repair itself, keeps your immune system strong enough to fight off illness, and even affects how well you concentrate and sleep. Athletes, chefs, and doctors all use exactly this knowledge every single day.
Real life: sports scientists plan athletes' meals around nutrient needs — extra carbohydrates before a big race for energy, extra protein afterward to help muscles repair. Doctors and dietitians use the same nutrient groups you're about to learn to help people recover from illness or grow up healthy.

The Main Nutrient Groups 🍞

Food contains several different NUTRIENTS — useful substances your body needs — and each one does a different main job.
Carbohydrates (bread, rice, pasta, potatoes): your body's main, fastest source of ENERGY.
Protein (meat, fish, eggs, beans, tofu): builds and repairs your body — muscles, skin, and more — and helps you GROW.
Fats (oils, butter, nuts, avocado): a longer-lasting store of energy, and helps protect your organs and keep you warm.
Vitamins & minerals (fruit, vegetables, dairy, and more): needed in tiny amounts, but each one has a specific, essential job — from healthy eyes to strong bones.
Fibre (whole grains, fruit, vegetables): doesn't get digested itself, but keeps your digestive system moving smoothly.
Water: not a food, but essential for every single process in your body, from digestion to keeping cool.

No single food group can do every one of these jobs — that's exactly why a healthy diet needs a MIX, not just a lot of one thing.

The Balanced Plate 🍽️

A BALANCED DIET means eating the right rough proportions of each nutrient group over time — not eating everything in equal amounts.
🍎🥦 Fruit & veg — biggest share, vitamins/minerals/fibre
🍞 Carbohydrates — main energy
🍗 Protein — growth & repair
🥛 Dairy — calcium for bones
🍬 Fats & sugary treats — smallest share
This is a simple model of a balanced plate — a big share of fruit and vegetables, a similarly large share of starchy carbohydrates, a smaller share of protein, a small share of dairy, and only a tiny share for fats and sugary treats. It's not about banning any food — it's about roughly how OFTEN and how MUCH of each group you eat over time.

What Does This Food Mainly Give You? 🌳

Is this food a fruit, vegetable, whole grain, bean, or lean meat/fish — mostly "whole," not heavily processed?
↓
YES — fruits, vegetables, whole grains, beans, lean meat/fish
Gives real nutrients your body needs — vitamins, minerals, fibre, protein, and/or steady energy. Eat these often — they're the foundation of a balanced diet.
NO — sugary sweets, fried snacks, sugary drinks
Mostly gives quick energy and little else your body actually needs. Fine as an occasional treat, not a main part of a balanced diet.
Key idea: this isn't about "good foods" and "bad foods" — treats can absolutely be part of a healthy diet in small amounts. It's about which foods you build most of your meals around, since those are the ones that need to carry the real nutritional workload.
