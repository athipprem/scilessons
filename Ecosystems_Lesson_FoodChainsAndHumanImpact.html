Quick Recall from Unit 4 🔁

🔁 Remember decomposers? You learned that decomposers (bacteria and fungi) break down dead plants and animals and recycle the nutrients back into the soil — nature's recyclers.

Decomposers are actually just ONE of three important jobs living things do in an ecosystem. Today you'll meet the other two, and see how decomposers fit into the bigger picture — including a surprising fact about the air itself.

Why learn this? 🤔

Farmers plan crops around what nutrients are in their soil, conservationists design nature reserves around food webs, and scientists track pollution and the greenhouse effect to protect ecosystems. Understanding how living things depend on each other — and on the air, water, and soil — is one of the biggest ideas in all of biology.
Real life: when one part of an ecosystem changes (a species disappears, a river gets polluted, the air warms up), it can affect everything connected to it — sometimes in surprising ways.

The Ecosystem Team: Three Jobs 🌳🦌♻️

Every living thing in an ecosystem has one of three main jobs: PRODUCER, CONSUMER, or DECOMPOSER.
🌳
Producers
make their OWN food using sunlight (plants, algae) — this is photosynthesis, from Unit 8
🦌
Consumers
CANNOT make their own food — they eat other living things (all animals)
🦠
Decomposers
break down DEAD things and recycle nutrients back to the soil (bacteria, fungi — from Unit 4)
Key idea: every single living thing in an ecosystem fits into exactly one of these three jobs — and every ecosystem needs all three working together, or the whole system breaks down.

Nutrients & The Air Around You 💨

Surprise: the air around you is NOT mostly oxygen — it's mostly a gas called NITROGEN.

About 4 out of every 5 parts of air (roughly 78%) is nitrogen. Only about 1 out of every 5 parts (roughly 21%) is oxygen, with tiny amounts of other gases mixed in.

Why this matters: plants need nitrogen to grow strong and healthy, almost like a vitamin. But plants CANNOT use the nitrogen gas floating in the air directly — it has to be changed into a different form first.

Special bacteria living in the soil (and in tiny lumps on the roots of some plants, like peas and beans) can "catch" nitrogen from the air and change it into a form that plant roots CAN use. This process is called nitrogen fixation.

Connecting the dots: this is exactly the same kind of invisible, essential job that decomposers do (Unit 4) — tiny living things in the soil, unseen but absolutely necessary, keeping nutrients flowing through the ecosystem so plants (producers) can keep growing.

A single food chain is simple, but in a real ecosystem, most living things eat MORE than one kind of food, and get eaten by MORE than one kind of predator. When you connect several food chains together, you get a food web — a much more realistic picture of how energy moves through an ecosystem.

Key idea: if one part of a food web disappears (say, all the rabbits), it doesn't just affect the fox — it can affect every other chain connected to rabbits too, since a real ecosystem is a web, not a single line.

Human Impact: Pollution & the Greenhouse Effect 🏭

Humans can harm ecosystems in TWO different ways: pollution, and the greenhouse effect.
🗑️ Pollution

Litter, harmful chemicals, or oil that gets into water, soil, or air can directly poison or kill living things — separate from anything to do with heat.

🌡️ Greenhouse Effect

Certain gases (like carbon dioxide) act like a blanket around the Earth, trapping heat from the Sun instead of letting it escape back into space — named after a greenhouse, a glass building that traps heat to keep plants warm.

Key idea: a little greenhouse effect is natural and keeps Earth warm enough for life. But burning too much fuel (cars, factories) releases extra carbon dioxide, thickening that "blanket" and making Earth warmer than it should be — part of what's called climate change.
