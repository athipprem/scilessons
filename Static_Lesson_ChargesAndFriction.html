Quick Fix from Unit 22 💡

Before Static Electricity, let's lock in two ideas from Nutrition that tripped up several questions last time.

Fix 1 — going from SYMPTOM back to NUTRIENT (the reverse direction).

Last time, when given a diet and asked "what will they run short on?", that was easy. But when given a person's SYMPTOMS and asked "what are they short on?", it was harder — and Calcium kept getting picked as a default guess, even when it didn't fit.

Here's a 3-step check to use every time you see symptoms first:

Step 1: Name the SYMPTOM exactly (e.g. "bleeding gums" or "feels out of breath, low energy carrying oxygen").
Step 2: Ask "which nutrient's JOB matches that symptom?" (Vitamin C's job = healthy gums/skin/healing. Iron's job = helping blood carry oxygen. Calcium's job = strong bones/teeth — a totally different symptom shape, like weak or aching bones.)
Step 3: ONLY THEN name the nutrient. Don't jump straight to a familiar-sounding name before checking the job matches.

Quick Check 1: A sailor on a long voyage develops bleeding gums, feels constantly tired, and his small cuts are healing very slowly. Using the 3-step check — which nutrient is he most likely short on?

✔ Right — bleeding gums, tiredness, and slow-healing cuts are the classic Vitamin C deficiency symptoms (scurvy).
✘ Run the 3 steps: what's the symptom, whose JOB matches it, then name the nutrient. Bleeding gums point to Vitamin C, not Calcium.

Quick Check 2: A doctor tells a patient their blood has trouble carrying enough oxygen around the body, leaving them breathless and weak. Which mineral is most likely missing?

✔ Correct — Iron's job is helping blood carry oxygen, so a low oxygen-carrying ability points straight to Iron.
✘ Which nutrient's JOB is "helping blood carry oxygen"? That's Iron, not Calcium — Calcium's job is bones and teeth, a different symptom shape entirely.

Fix 2 — a fair test can show NO difference, and that's still a real, useful result.

Every fair test you've seen so far had a clear, visible difference in the outcome. But sometimes a fair test is run correctly and the two groups end up looking exactly the same — that doesn't mean the test failed or was unfair. It means the thing you changed probably doesn't make a difference under those conditions. A "no difference" result is just as real and useful as a "big difference" result — it still answers the question.

Quick Check 3: Two identical seedlings are grown in the same soil, light, and water — except one is watered with a sugar-water solution and the other with plain water. After two weeks, both seedlings look exactly the same height and colour. What is the BEST conclusion?

✔ Exactly — only one thing changed (the water type), so it WAS a fair test, and "no visible difference" is a real, valid result.
✘ Only one thing was changed (the water), so this WAS a fair test — a fair test showing no difference is still a valid, useful answer.

Quick Recall from Unit 6 🔁

🔁 Remember circuits? Back in Unit 6 you learned that electricity in a circuit is really tiny particles called ELECTRONS flowing around a loop of wire. Electrons carry a NEGATIVE charge.

Today those same electrons show up again — but instead of flowing neatly around a wire, they'll jump from ONE object straight onto another when you rub them together. That's what "static" electricity means: charge that STAYS PUT on an object, instead of flowing through a circuit.

Why learn this? 🤔

Static electricity is why your hair stands up after pulling off a wooly hat, why laundry crackles and sticks together fresh out of the dryer, why you sometimes get a small shock touching a metal doorknob, and — on a much bigger scale — it's exactly what causes lightning during a thunderstorm.
Real life: photocopiers and laser printers use static electricity to stick tiny particles of ink onto paper in exactly the right pattern, and fuel trucks carry a metal grounding strap that drags along the road specifically to drain away static charge, so a spark can't ignite the fuel while it's being pumped.

Charges Inside Everything ⚛️

Every object is built from tiny particles that carry an electric charge — some POSITIVE, some NEGATIVE.
➕
Protons
carry a POSITIVE charge — locked deep inside the object, they never move during rubbing
➖
Electrons
carry a NEGATIVE charge — sit on the outside, and CAN be rubbed off onto another object
Key idea: normally, an object has an EQUAL number of protons and electrons, so its charges balance out and the object is NEUTRAL (no overall charge). Static electricity happens when rubbing moves some electrons from one object to another, so the two objects are no longer balanced.

Charging by Friction 🎈

Rubbing a balloon on hair transfers electrons FROM the hair ONTO the balloon. The balloon ends up with extra electrons (NEGATIVE), and the hair ends up short of electrons (POSITIVE).
RULE: whichever object GAINS electrons becomes NEGATIVELY charged. Whichever object LOSES electrons becomes POSITIVELY charged. The total number of protons never changes — only electrons move.
Key idea: friction (rubbing) doesn't CREATE any new charge out of nothing — it just moves existing electrons from one object to the other. That's why the two rubbed objects always end up with OPPOSITE charges: one gained what the other lost.

Which Charge Did It End Up With? 🌳

After rubbing, did this object GAIN electrons, or LOSE electrons?
Object ended up with MORE electrons than protons
Negatively charged (−)
Object ended up with FEWER electrons than protons
Positively charged (+)
How to use this: in any rubbing pair, one object gains electrons (goes negative) and the other loses the exact same electrons (goes positive) — they always end up as a matched, opposite pair. If you know one object's charge, you automatically know the other one is the opposite.
