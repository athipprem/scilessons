Small Amounts, Big Jobs 🗺️

MAP: vitamins and minerals are only needed in tiny amounts, but going without one for too long can make you seriously ill — as one famous ship full of sailors found out the hard way.

Lesson 1 introduced vitamins and minerals as one of the six nutrient groups. Today you'll meet the most important ones by name, find out which foods give you each one, and uncover the real historical story of a disease that puzzled sailors for centuries — until someone ran one of the first fair-test experiments in history to solve it.

Quick Recall from Lesson 1 🔁

🔁 Remember the nutrient groups? Lesson 1 covered six groups: carbohydrates (energy), protein (growth & repair), fats (energy store), vitamins & minerals (tiny amounts, specific jobs), fibre (keeps digestion moving), and water. You also learned that fruit and vegetables make up the biggest share of a balanced plate.

Today's question: WHY do fruit and vegetables get such a big share of the plate, when they don't have much energy or protein compared to bread or meat? The answer is exactly the nutrient group you're about to explore.

Why learn this? 🤔

Vitamins and minerals keep your eyes, bones, blood, skin, and immune system working properly. Going without enough of a specific one for a long time can cause a real, sometimes serious, deficiency disease — something doctors, nutritionists, and even ship captains throughout history have had to understand and prevent.
Real life: doctors test patients' blood for vitamin and mineral levels to diagnose deficiencies. Food companies add extra vitamins to products like milk and cereal (called "fortifying") specifically to help prevent common deficiencies in the population.

Meet the Key Vitamins & Minerals 🔑

Each VITAMIN and MINERAL has its own specific job in your body — getting enough of a wide variety is what matters, not just eating a lot of food overall.
Vitamin A
Keeps your eyes healthy, especially seeing in dim light. Found in carrots, sweet potatoes, spinach.
Vitamin C
Keeps your immune system, skin, and gums healthy, and helps wounds heal. Found in oranges, lemons, tomatoes, peppers.
Vitamin D
Helps your body absorb calcium for strong bones. Your skin makes it using sunlight, and it's also found in eggs and oily fish.
Calcium (a mineral)
Builds strong bones and teeth. Found in milk, cheese, yoghurt, leafy greens.
Iron (a mineral)
Helps your blood carry oxygen around your body. Found in red meat, beans, leafy greens.
Fibre-friendly note
Many vitamin-C-rich foods (citrus, peppers) are also fibre-rich — one more reason fruit and veg get such a big plate share.

Vitamin C: A Closer Look 🍊

Citrus fruits (oranges, lemons), tomatoes, and peppers are all rich sources of Vitamin C.
Key idea: Vitamin C keeps your immune system strong, keeps your gums and skin healthy, and helps cuts and wounds heal. Because your body can't store much Vitamin C, you need a fresh supply regularly, from food — which becomes a real problem for anyone who can't get fresh fruit or vegetables for a long time.

The Real Story of Scurvy ⚓

In the 1700s, sailors on long ocean voyages often had no fresh fruit or vegetables for months — and many developed a disease called SCURVY: bleeding gums, extreme tiredness, and wounds that wouldn't heal.
Ship surgeon James Lind wanted to find out what actually cured scurvy. In 1747, he ran one of the first FAIR-TEST medical experiments ever recorded: he split 12 sick sailors into pairs, gave each pair a different treatment (vinegar, seawater, citrus fruit, and others) — keeping everything else about their conditions the same — and tracked who got better.
Key idea: the pair given ORANGES AND LEMONS recovered dramatically faster than every other pair. Lind's fair test proved that citrus fruit — rich in Vitamin C — cured scurvy, even though nobody yet knew vitamins existed as a scientific idea. This is exactly the fair-test thinking you practiced back in earlier units: change only ONE thing (the treatment) and keep everything else the same, so you can trust what the evidence shows.
