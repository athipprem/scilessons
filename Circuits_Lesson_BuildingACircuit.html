Why learn this? 🤔

Torches, doorbells, toy cars, remote controls, and phone chargers all rely on circuits. Every one of them needs a complete, unbroken path for electricity to flow — break that path anywhere and the whole thing stops working. ASMOPSS loves circuit diagrams: you'll be asked to look at a drawing and say whether the bulb lights up, and why.
Real life: a torch with a flat battery, a loose wire, or its switch left "off" all fail for the exact same underlying reason — the loop isn't complete. Understanding WHY a circuit works means you can diagnose WHY it doesn't.

What Makes a Complete Circuit? 🔁

A circuit needs: a POWER SOURCE (battery), a PATH (wires), and something to USE the energy (like a bulb) — all connected in one unbroken loop, with no gaps.

Follow the loop with your finger: battery → wire → bulb → wire → back to battery. No gaps anywhere = a complete circuit = the bulb lights up.

Does the loop go all the way around, battery → wire → bulb → wire → battery, with NO gaps?
↓
YES
Circuit is COMPLETE
the bulb lights up 💡
NO (a gap anywhere)
Circuit is INCOMPLETE
the bulb stays dark
(loose wire, flat battery, broken bulb, or open switch)
Key idea: it doesn't matter WHERE the gap is — one missing connection anywhere in the loop stops the whole circuit, not just the part near the gap.

Switches: Open vs Closed 🔘

A switch is a DELIBERATE gap you can open or close on purpose — that's how you turn things on and off.
⭕ OPEN switch
the gap is broken on purpose
→ circuit INCOMPLETE
→ bulb OFF
✅ CLOSED switch
the gap is joined up
→ circuit COMPLETE
→ bulb ON
Key fact: "open" and "closed" describe the SWITCH, not the room! An open switch breaks the loop (like a drawbridge up); a closed switch completes it (drawbridge down, traffic can cross).
Try it in your head: a torch has its switch flicked to "off." Using the words from today, is that switch open or closed, and is the circuit complete or incomplete? (Open switch → incomplete circuit → bulb off.)

What Kind of Energy Is This? ⚡

Energy doesn't appear from nowhere — a battery starts as CHEMICAL energy, and the circuit transfers it into other forms.
🔋 CHEMICAL
stored inside the battery
→
⚡ ELECTRICAL
flowing around the complete circuit
→
💡🔥 LIGHT + HEAT
given out by the bulb
Key idea: the battery's job is to store chemical energy and release it as electrical energy once the circuit is complete. The bulb's job is to change that electrical energy into light (what you want) and a little heat (a side effect, which is why a bulb feels warm).
Same chain, different gadget: a battery-powered buzzer changes electrical energy into sound instead of light — but it always STARTS the same way, as chemical energy stored in the battery.

1. A circuit has a battery, two wires, and a bulb, but one wire is disconnected at one end. What happens?

✔ Right — one gap anywhere breaks the whole loop, so no electricity flows at all.
✘ Think about the loop rule: any gap, anywhere, stops the WHOLE circuit — not just makes it weaker.

2. A switch is flicked so that its two metal contacts are pulled apart, leaving a gap. What is the switch, and what happens to the bulb?

✔ Correct — pulling the contacts apart makes a gap, so the switch is OPEN and the circuit is incomplete.
✘ A gap in the switch means it is OPEN. An open switch always means an incomplete circuit.

3. What form of energy is stored inside a battery BEFORE it is connected in a circuit?

✔ Right — a battery stores chemical energy, which becomes electrical energy once the circuit is complete.
✘ Electrical energy only starts flowing once the circuit is complete. Before that, the battery just stores chemical energy.

4. In a working circuit, a lit bulb feels slightly warm to touch. This shows that the bulb is changing electrical energy into:

✔ Exactly — a bulb gives out both light (what we want) and heat (a side effect), which is why it feels warm.
✘ The warmth is a clue — the bulb isn't only making light, it's also giving out heat energy.
