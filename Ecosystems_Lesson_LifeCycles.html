Quick Recall from Unit 1 & Unit 8 🔁

🔁 Remember flowers? Back in Unit 1 you learned a flower's main job is to make seeds. In Unit 8 you learned leaves make food using sunlight, water, and air (photosynthesis).

Today you'll go much deeper: exactly HOW a flower makes seeds, part by part, and exactly what a seed needs before it can even start growing into a new plant.

Why learn this? 🤔

Farmers and gardeners plan exactly when to plant seeds based on what they need to sprout. Scientists breeding new crops need to understand flower parts to control pollination. And every fruit or vegetable you eat exists because of this exact cycle repeating, generation after generation.
Real life: a farmer checks soil temperature and moisture before planting, not just the calendar date — because germination depends on specific conditions being right, not just "it's spring."

What Does a Seed Need to Sprout? 🌱

A seed needs exactly THREE things to germinate (start sprouting): WATER, the right WARMTH (temperature), and AIR (oxygen).
💧
Water
softens the seed coat and wakes up the tiny plant inside
🌡️
Warmth
too cold or too hot and the seed stays dormant (asleep)
💨
Air (oxygen)
the tiny plant inside needs oxygen to release energy from its stored food
Surprise: a seed does NOT need soil or light to germinate! A seed already has a tiny store of food packed inside it, enough to power the very first sprouting. That's why the classic "bean in a wet paper towel" experiment works — no soil at all, just a damp towel, warmth, and air.
🚫🪴
NOT needed to germinate
Soil (the seed's own stored food is enough at first)
🚫☀️
NOT needed to germinate
Light (needed LATER for photosynthesis, once it's a growing seedling)

Inside a Flower: Every Part's Job 🌸

A flower has TWO main sets of parts: parts that make POLLEN, and parts that receive pollen and make seeds.
Pollen-making side (stamen): the filament is the thin stalk; the anther sits on top and makes powdery pollen.
Seed-making side (pistil): the stigma is the sticky top that catches pollen; the style is the tube pollen travels down; the ovary is the base, and it contains the ovule(s) — the egg cells that become seeds.
Outer parts: petals are the colourful parts that attract insects; sepals are the small green leaf-like parts that protected the flower bud before it opened.

Pollination → Fertilization → New Seeds 🐝

Pollen must travel from an ANTHER to a STIGMA before a new seed can form.
🐝 Insect (or wind) carries pollen from the anther
→
🌸 Pollen lands on a STIGMA (pollination)
→
⬇️ Pollen travels down the STYLE
→
🥚 Pollen joins an OVULE inside the ovary (fertilization)
→
🌰 The ovule becomes a NEW SEED
Key idea: pollination (pollen landing on a stigma) and fertilization (pollen actually joining an ovule) are two different steps — pollination has to happen FIRST, but it doesn't guarantee fertilization will follow.
🍅 Once fertilized, the ovary often grows into a fruit around the new seeds, protecting them and helping them spread — completing the loop back to Screen 4's seed germination.

YES to all three
Will germinate (soil and light not required yet)
Missing even ONE

🚫 Spot the flaw: A student says: "This seed has been sitting in a dry packet on a shelf for months. It looks small, hard, and completely still — it must not be a living thing at all."

A dry seed is still ALIVE — it's just dormant (like it's asleep), waiting for water, warmth, and air before it starts actively growing again. Looking still and lifeless is not the same as actually being dead; the seed is a living thing in a resting state.
