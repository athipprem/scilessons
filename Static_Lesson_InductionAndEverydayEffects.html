Quick Recall from Lesson 1 🔁

🔁 Remember charges and friction? Every object has protons (+) and electrons (−). Rubbing two objects together transfers electrons from one to the other — the one that GAINS electrons goes negative, the one that LOSES electrons goes positive. Like charges (+/+ or −/−) REPEL each other; unlike charges (+/−) ATTRACT each other.

Today you'll use that "like repels, unlike attracts" rule to explain something that seems strange at first: how a charged object can attract something that isn't charged at all.

Why learn this? 🤔

This exact idea — a charge pulling on something neutral without touching it — is how photocopiers and laser printers place ink precisely, how air purifiers called "electrostatic precipitators" pull dust out of the air, and it's the very first step of every single lightning bolt you'll ever see.
Real life: rub a plastic comb on your hair and hold it near small scraps of paper or a thin, steady stream of water from a tap — both the paper and the water will bend TOWARD the comb, even though neither one is charged. That's electrostatic induction in action.

Attracting Without Touching 📎

A charged object can push and pull the electrons INSIDE a nearby neutral object, even without touching it — this is called INDUCTION (or polarisation).
The negatively charged balloon pushes the paper's own electrons toward the FAR side (they're both negative, so they repel). This leaves the NEAR side of the paper slightly positive — which is then attracted to the negative balloon.

Step by step:

1. The paper starts neutral (equal + and −, evenly spread).
2. The charged balloon comes close — its charge REPELS the same charge inside the paper, pushing those electrons to the far side.
3. Now the near side of the paper (facing the balloon) has slightly MORE positive charge showing, and the far side has slightly MORE negative charge.
4. Opposite charges attract — so the paper's near (positive-leaning) side is pulled toward the balloon's negative charge, and the paper moves.

Key idea: NO electrons actually jump from the balloon to the paper — nothing is touching, and the paper is still overall neutral. The electrons just SHIFT POSITION inside the paper (this shifting is called polarisation). That's what makes induction different from charging by friction, where electrons really do transfer permanently from one object to the other.

Static Electricity All Around You 🎈

🎈
Balloon on a wall
a rubbed balloon sticks to a wall — induction pulls the wall's electrons out of position, just like the paper
👕
Static cling
clothes tumbling in a dryer rub together, transfer electrons by friction, and end up sticking together (opposite charges attract)
💇
Hair standing up
pulling off a wooly hat charges each hair the SAME way — since like charges repel, the hairs push apart from each other
🚪
Doorknob shock
walking on carpet builds up charge on you by friction; touching a metal doorknob lets that charge jump across as a tiny spark
Key idea: "hair standing up" uses the LIKE-CHARGES-REPEL rule (Lesson 1), while "balloon sticking to a wall" uses INDUCTION (this lesson) — two different static electricity effects that can look similar but work in different ways. Static cling and the doorknob shock both use plain charging-by-friction, just like Lesson 1's balloon-and-hair example.

Lightning: Static Electricity on a Giant Scale ⛈️

Inside the storm cloud, ice crystals and water droplets rub against each other in strong updrafts — just like rubbing a balloon on hair — building up a large negative charge low in the cloud. This induces a positive charge on the ground below. When the pull between them gets strong enough, a lightning bolt discharges the built-up charge in one giant spark.
Key idea: lightning uses BOTH ideas from this unit — friction charging (ice and water droplets rubbing inside the cloud) AND induction (the cloud's negative charge pulling positive charge to the surface below, without touching it) — just at a much, much bigger scale than a balloon and hair.

Friction, or Induction? 🌳

Are the two objects actually TOUCHING and being rubbed together, or is the charged object nearby but NOT touching?
Objects rub together directly (e.g. balloon on hair, clothes in a dryer)
Charging by friction — electrons actually transfer, charge stays after separating
A charged object is held NEAR a neutral object, not touching it (e.g. balloon near paper, cloud above ground)
Induction — electrons only SHIFT position, no permanent transfer
How to use this: ask "did the two objects actually rub together?" first. If yes, it's friction charging and the charge is now permanent. If a charged object simply got close to something without touching, it's induction — a temporary shift, not a transfer.
