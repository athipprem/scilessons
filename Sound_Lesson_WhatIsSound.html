Quick Recall from Unit 2 🔁

🔁 States of matter check: back in Unit 2, you learned that solids have their particles packed TIGHTLY together, liquids have particles more loosely arranged, and gases have particles spread FAR apart from each other.

Here's the connection: today (and especially in Lesson 2) you'll discover that this exact particle spacing is the reason sound travels at very different speeds through solids, liquids, and gases — tightly packed particles pass a vibration along much faster than spread-out ones.

Why learn this? 🤔

Understanding how sound travels explains why you can hear a train coming by putting your ear to the track before you can hear it in the air, why astronauts in space can't talk to each other just by shouting, why dolphins and whales communicate over huge distances underwater, and why a doctor's stethoscope works.
Real life: submarine sonar bounces sound through water to find objects, doctors listen to your heartbeat through a stethoscope, musicians tune instruments by ear, and engineers design concert halls so sound reaches every seat clearly.

What Is Sound? 🥁

Sound is created by VIBRATIONS — something moving quickly back and forth — which push and pull on the particles around it.
When the drum skin vibrates (moves back and forth very fast), it pushes and pulls the air particles next to it. Those particles bump the next ones, and so on — a wave of squeezed-together particles (a "compression wave") spreads outward until it reaches your ear.
Key idea: every single sound — a voice, a guitar string, a slamming door — starts with something vibrating. No vibration, no sound.

Sound Needs "Something" to Travel Through 🌫️

Sound can ONLY travel through a MEDIUM — matter such as a solid, liquid, or gas. Sound CANNOT travel through a vacuum (completely empty space, with no particles at all).

Think back to how sound spreads: it's particles bumping into other particles, passing the vibration along like a line of falling dominoes. If there are no particles at all — a true vacuum, like the empty space between stars — there's nothing to bump into, so the vibration can't travel anywhere.

🔊 Light
  • Can travel through empty space (a vacuum)
  • This is how sunlight reaches Earth across empty space
🔇 Sound
  • CANNOT travel through empty space
  • This is why space is completely silent — astronauts can't hear explosions or hear each other shout across open space
Key idea: light and sound behave differently here — light travels fine through a vacuum (that's how sunlight reaches us), but sound absolutely needs matter — a solid, liquid, or gas — to travel through at all.

Can Sound Travel Here? 🔍

Ask this one key question before predicting.
Is there a medium present (a solid, liquid, or gas — any matter at all)?
YES
Sound CAN travel through it, vibrating particle to particle.
NO (a true vacuum)
Sound CANNOT travel at all — there's nothing to carry the vibration.
Key idea: this rule applies no matter what the medium actually is — air, water, wood, metal, all work, because they're all made of particles close enough together to pass a vibration along.
