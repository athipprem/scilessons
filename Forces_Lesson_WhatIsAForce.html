Quick Recall from Unit 9 🧪

🔁 Reactions check: in Unit 9 you learned about CHANGES — physical changes (same substance, different look or state) and chemical changes (brand new substances form).

Today's topic is about a different kind of change: changes in MOVEMENT. Something can speed up, slow down, start moving, stop, or change direction — and every single one of those changes is caused by a FORCE. Same idea as Unit 9 (something changes, and there's always a reason why), just applied to motion instead of substances.

Why learn this? 🤔

Forces explain almost everything that moves (or doesn't move) around you — why a ball stops rolling, why you need a seatbelt, why a dropped pencil falls instead of floating, why ice is slippery. Understanding forces is the foundation for understanding sports, vehicles, machines, and even why astronauts float in space.
Real life: engineers design car brakes to create enough force to stop a moving car safely. Athletes use their leg muscles to create a pushing force against the ground to jump higher. Playground swing designers rely on gravity and pushing forces to make swings work safely.

What Is a Force? 👋

A FORCE is simply a PUSH or a PULL. A force can make something START moving, STOP moving, SPEED UP, SLOW DOWN, or CHANGE DIRECTION.
Key idea: pushing a stationary ball makes it START moving; kicking a rolling ball harder makes it SPEED UP; catching a rolling ball makes it STOP; a gentle tap can change which direction it rolls. All five of these — start, stop, speed up, slow down, change direction — are things ONLY a force can do.

Contact vs Non-Contact Forces 🤝

A CONTACT force needs the objects to be touching. A NON-CONTACT force works even when the objects are NOT touching.
CONTACT forces
pushing, pulling, friction, air resistance, water resistance
the objects are touching
NON-CONTACT forces
gravity, magnetism
the objects are NOT touching
Key idea: you have to actually touch a door to push it open (contact). But a dropped pencil falls to the floor even though nothing is touching it and pulling it down — that's gravity, a non-contact force. A magnet can pull a paperclip toward it before they even touch — that's magnetism, also non-contact (you learned about this back in Unit 3!).

Is This a Contact or Non-Contact Force? 🔍

Use this checklist to sort any force you see or read about.
Are the two objects actually TOUCHING each other while the force acts?
↓
YES
CONTACT force
e.g. kicking a ball, friction, air resistance on a parachute
NO
NON-CONTACT force
e.g. gravity pulling a dropped object down, a magnet attracting from a distance
Key idea: a parachute slowing a skydiver down is a CONTACT force (air is physically touching and pushing against the parachute fabric) — even though it doesn't feel like "touching" in the everyday sense, the air and parachute really are in contact. Gravity pulling the skydiver down the whole time, though, is non-contact — Earth isn't touching them at all.
