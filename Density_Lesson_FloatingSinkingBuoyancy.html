Quick Recall from Unit 12 🔁

🔁 Floating & sinking check: back in Unit 12, you learned that some materials float in water but sink in cooking oil — like beeswax, which is denser than water but less dense than oil. You already knew WHICH liquid each material floated or sank in.

Here's the connection: today you'll learn the actual RULE that explains WHY — comparing an object's density directly to the liquid's density tells you exactly whether it will float or sink, in ANY liquid, not just water and oil.

Why learn this? 🤔

Understanding the density-vs-liquid rule explains why massive cargo ships stay afloat, why some fish can hover perfectly still in water, why oil spills always float on the ocean's surface instead of sinking, and why a life jacket keeps a swimmer's head above water.
Real life: engineers use this exact rule to design ships and submarines, oceanographers track floating oil spills to predict where they'll drift, and swimmers understand why salty ocean water helps them float more easily than fresh water (salty water is denser).

The Float or Sink Rule 💧

Compare the OBJECT's density to the LIQUID's density: less dense than the liquid = floats. More dense than the liquid = sinks.
The cork is LESS dense than water, so it floats on top. The coin is MORE dense than water, so it sinks straight to the bottom.
Key idea: it's always a COMPARISON — an object doesn't just "float" or "sink" on its own, it floats or sinks compared to whatever liquid it's placed in. The exact same object could float in one liquid and sink in a different, less dense liquid.
This rule isn't just for solids in liquids: it works for ANY two substances being compared this way — a liquid poured into another liquid (like oil floating on water), or even a gas surrounded by another gas. A helium balloon rises up through the air for exactly the same reason a cork rises up through water: helium is less dense than the air around it, so it "floats" upward through it, just like cork floats upward through water.
Comparing several floating objects: if TWO different objects both float, the one that floats HIGHER out of the water (with less of it underwater) is the LESS dense one, and the one that floats LOWER (with more of it underwater, closer to sinking) is the MORE dense one — even though both are less dense than water. The lower an object sits, the closer it is to being as dense as the water itself.

What You SEE vs What You WORK OUT 🔍

An OBSERVATION is something you can directly see, hear, or measure. An INFERENCE is a conclusion you WORK OUT based on that observation.

This is one of the most important skills in all of science — and floating/sinking experiments are a perfect place to practise it.

👀 Observation (directly seen)
  • "The block floated on the surface."
  • "The coin sank to the bottom."
  • "Bubbles rose up from the object."
🧠 Inference (worked out)
  • "So the block must be less dense than water."
  • "So the coin must be denser than water."
  • "So the object might have trapped air inside it."
Key idea: "it floated" is something you SAW happen. "It must be less dense than water" is a conclusion you WORKED OUT from what you saw — it's not something you directly observed with your eyes. Mixing these two up is a really common science-investigation mistake.

Will It Float or Sink? 🔍

Ask this one key question before predicting.
Is the object's density LESS THAN or MORE THAN the liquid's density?
LESS THAN
The object FLOATS on top of the liquid.
MORE THAN
The object SINKS to the bottom of the liquid.
Key idea: if you don't know the exact density numbers, you can also work backwards — WATCHING whether something floats or sinks (an observation) lets you INFER whether it's more or less dense than the liquid.

Worked Example: Testing an Unknown Block 🧪

A scientist has an unknown solid block and wants to predict whether it will float or sink in water.

  1. Step 1: She measures the block's mass: 340g.
  2. Step 2: She measures its volume (by how much water it displaces): 200 cm³.
  3. Step 3: She compares this to an equal 200 cm³ of pure water, which has a mass of only 200g.
  4. Step 4: Since the block (340g) has much MORE mass than the same volume of water (200g), the block must be MORE dense than water.
  5. Step 5: Prediction: the block will SINK when placed in the water. She tests it — and it sinks, confirming the prediction.
🚫 Spot the flaw: A student says: "I watched the block sink, so I directly SAW that it is denser than water." What's wrong with this statement?

The student directly OBSERVED the block sinking — that part is fine. But "it is denser than water" is something worked out (inferred) from that observation, not something seen directly with the eyes. You can't look at an object and see its density number — you only see behaviour (floating or sinking) and then reason about what that behaviour tells you.
