Quick Recall from Unit 4 🔁

🔁 Microorganisms check: back in Unit 4, you learned that bacteria are tiny living things far too small to see without a microscope.

Here's the connection: bacteria are actually a perfect example of a unicellular organism — their entire body is just ONE single cell, doing every job (eating, moving, sensing, reproducing) all by itself.

Why learn this? 🤔

Understanding unicellular vs multicellular life explains how a single bacterium can cause an illness, why your body needs so many different types of cells to function, and how doctors and scientists study everything from simple microorganisms to complex human tissue.
Real life: doctors studying an infection need to know whether they're dealing with a simple unicellular bacterium or a more complex multicellular parasite, since they're treated very differently. Biologists studying the human body rely on understanding how trillions of specialized cells cooperate to keep a person alive and healthy.

One Cell, or Many? 🔬

A UNICELLULAR organism's entire body is ONE single cell. A MULTICELLULAR organism's body is made of many cells working together.
🦠 Unicellular (one cell does every job)
  • Bacteria
  • Amoeba
  • Some yeast
🌳 Multicellular (many specialized cells)
  • Plants
  • Animals
  • Most fungi (like mushrooms)
Key idea: a unicellular organism's single cell must handle every single job needed to survive, all by itself — a multicellular organism instead spreads those jobs across many different, specialized cells.

Specialized Cells: Shaped for the Job 💪

A multicellular organism isn't just lots of copies of the same cell — different cells are shaped differently because they do different jobs.
Nerve cells
long and thin, to carry messages quickly over long distances
Muscle cells
able to contract (shorten), to create movement
Red blood cells
flattened disc shape with no nucleus, to carry more oxygen
Root hair cells (in plants)
long and thin, to soak up more water from soil
Key idea: this is exactly like Unit 11's body systems — just as your digestive, respiratory, and circulatory systems each have their own organs built for their own job, a multicellular body has many different cell shapes, each one perfectly built for its own specific task.

Unicellular or Multicellular? 🔍

Ask this one key question about any living thing.
Is this living thing's entire body made of just ONE single cell, doing every job by itself?
YES
It's UNICELLULAR — like a bacterium or an amoeba.
NO
It's MULTICELLULAR — made of many specialized cells working as a team, like a plant or an animal.
Key idea: the test isn't about size — it's about whether ONE cell does everything, or many DIFFERENT cells share the jobs between them.
