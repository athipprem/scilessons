Quick Fix: Iron — A New Way to Picture It 🩸🚚

Picture this: iron atoms are like tiny DELIVERY TRUCKS inside your blood. Their ONE job is picking up oxygen from your lungs and delivering it to every part of your body.
The delivery-truck picture: Imagine your blood is a delivery company, and iron atoms are the trucks. Every truck's job is exactly the same: drive to the lungs, pick up a load of oxygen, then deliver it to your muscles, brain, and every other part of your body that needs it to work properly. If you don't have enough iron, you don't have enough delivery trucks — so oxygen doesn't get delivered properly, no matter how much of it is sitting at the "warehouse" (your lungs). That's exactly why low iron makes you feel TIRED (your muscles aren't getting enough oxygen delivered to work well) and look PALE (your blood carries less oxygen-rich red colour around when there aren't enough "trucks" doing their job).
Why this matters: the mineral is Iron. The job is carrying oxygen. Every time you see tiredness + paleness in a question, picture the delivery trucks running low — that's Iron, every time.

Why learn this? 🤔

Speed is everywhere: a speedometer tells a driver how fast their car is going, athletes and coaches use speed to compare runners and swimmers, and pilots and ship captains calculate speed to work out exactly when they'll arrive somewhere.
Real life: when you check a maps app before a trip and it tells you "45 minutes to arrive," it's using exactly the speed formula you're about to learn — distance divided by an assumed average speed.

What Is Speed? 🏎️

SPEED = DISTANCE ÷ TIME. It tells you how much distance something covers in a certain amount of time.
Worked example: A car travels 100 kilometres in 2 hours. Speed = 100 ÷ 2 = 50 km/h (kilometres per hour). This means the car covers 50 kilometres for every hour it drives, on average.
Units: speed is usually measured in km/h (kilometres per hour) for cars and long trips, or m/s (metres per second) for shorter, faster things like a sprinter or a ball being thrown.
Key idea: the SAME distance covered in LESS time means a HIGHER speed. The SAME distance covered in MORE time means a LOWER speed. Always say this relationship out loud before answering a speed question — it's an easy thing to accidentally flip under a fast read.

Comparing Speeds ⚖️

To compare who (or what) is faster, work out EACH speed using the same formula, then compare the actual numbers — don't just guess from distance or time alone.
Worked example: A cheetah runs 30 metres in 2 seconds (30 ÷ 2 = 15 m/s). A greyhound runs 40 metres in 4 seconds (40 ÷ 4 = 10 m/s). Even though the greyhound covered MORE distance, the cheetah is actually FASTER, because it covered its distance in much less time. This is exactly why you can't compare speeds just by looking at distance or time on their own — you always need to calculate the actual speed for each one.
Trap to watch for: a runner who takes MORE time to finish the exact same race distance is the SLOWER one, not the faster one — more time for the same distance always means less speed.

Which Way Round Do I Use the Formula? 🌳

Do you already have BOTH the distance travelled AND the time it took?
YES
Speed = Distance ÷ Time
NO — comparing two speeds instead
Work out EACH speed separately first (Distance ÷ Time for each), then compare the two numbers
How to use this: always check what the question actually GIVES you first — distance and time together mean you can calculate speed directly; two separate distance/time pairs mean you calculate each speed first, then compare.
