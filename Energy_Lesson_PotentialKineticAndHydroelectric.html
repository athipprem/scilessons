Quick Recall from Lesson 1 & Unit 10 🔁

🔁 Remember Lesson 1? You learned potential energy is STORED energy, and that a transformation chain shows one form changing into another — like potential energy changing into kinetic energy when a ball drops.
🔁 Remember Unit 10? You learned that GRAVITY pulls every object toward the Earth. That's exactly the force that turns height into stored energy — the higher up something is, the more gravity "wants" to pull it down, and the more energy is stored up there.

Today you'll combine both ideas: gravity + height = gravitational potential energy, and you'll see this exact idea powering real hydroelectric dams.

Why learn this? 🤔

Roller coaster designers use gravitational potential energy to plan every hill and drop, without needing any engine once the coaster reaches the top. Hydroelectric power stations use the exact same idea at a massive scale — storing water high up so gravity can do the work of generating electricity for entire cities.
Real life: engineers deliberately build the tallest hill FIRST on a roller coaster — that's where the cart is given the most potential energy, which then powers every twist, turn, and smaller hill for the rest of the ride.

Gravitational Potential Energy ⛰️

The HIGHER an object is lifted, the MORE gravitational potential energy it has stored.
📕
Book on a low shelf
a small drop if it fell — LESS stored energy
📗
Same book, on a tall bookcase
a big drop if it fell — MORE stored energy
Key idea: a heavier object at the same height also stores MORE potential energy than a lighter one — both height AND weight (mass) affect how much energy is stored, but height alone is enough to compare two identical objects.

Potential Energy Becomes Kinetic Energy 🏃

As something FALLS, its potential energy transforms into kinetic energy — it loses height but gains speed.
⛰️ High up, not moving yet
(MOST potential energy)
→
🏃 Falling, gaining speed
(potential energy changing to kinetic)
→
💨 Just before landing
(MOST kinetic energy, LEAST potential energy)
Key idea: on a roller coaster, the cart has its highest potential energy at the very top of the tallest hill, and its highest kinetic energy (fastest speed) at the very bottom of that same hill — the height lost becomes exactly the speed gained.

Hydroelectric Power: Falling Water → Electricity 🌊

A hydroelectric dam stores water HIGH UP on purpose, so gravity can turn its potential energy into electricity.
🖱️ Tap a numbered dot on the diagram (or a row in the list below) to explore each part!
1Reservoir — the lake of water stored HIGH UP behind the dam; this height is what gives the water its gravitational potential energy.
2Dam Wall — the huge solid wall that holds the reservoir's water back until it's needed.
3Penstock — the large pipe that lets water fall from the reservoir down toward the turbine, turning potential energy into kinetic energy on the way down.
4Turbine — a wheel with blades that the falling water spins as it rushes past.
5Generator — connected to the spinning turbine; it turns that spinning (kinetic energy) into electrical energy.
Full chain: gravitational potential energy (water stored high in the reservoir) → kinetic energy (water falling and rushing through the penstock) → kinetic energy (the turbine spinning) → electrical energy (the generator) — directly answering "where does hydroelectric electricity actually come from?"

🚫 Spot the flaw: A student watches a roller coaster cart climb slowly to the top of the tallest hill, pause for a moment, then rush down the other side. She says: "The cart has the same amount of total energy at the top and at the bottom — it just LOOKS different because sometimes it's potential and sometimes it's kinetic."

This is actually CORRECT, not a flaw — it's the conservation-of-energy idea from Lesson 1! At the top, almost all the cart's energy is potential (barely moving). At the bottom, almost all of that same energy has transformed into kinetic (fast motion). The total amount of energy stays the same; only its FORM changes.
