Quick Recall from Unit 1 🌿

🔁 Remember leaves? A leaf in warm water gives off tiny bubbles — that's air escaping from the tiny holes and air spaces inside the leaf. Air isn't nothing — it's real, and it's matter!

That's the perfect bridge into today's lesson: gases (like air) are matter too — even though you can't see them, they take up space and they have mass. Keep that leaf-bubble idea in mind; we'll build on it.

Mini self-check: in Unit 1, were those leaf bubbles (1) food, or (2) air escaping from tiny holes? (Answer: air escaping — if that surprised you, that's OK, today makes it click for good.)

Why learn this? 🤔

Ever wondered why a balloon feels different full vs empty? Or why ice, water, and steam are secretly the same stuff? Understanding matter explains both — and it's one of the most-tested ideas in ASMOPSS Science.
🧊 Ice cube = solid 💧 Melted ice = liquid ☁️ Steam = gas 🎈 Balloon = full of gas matter
Your superpower: once you know what matter really is, you can explain why a full balloon weighs more than an empty one, why ice floats, and why sugar doesn't vanish when it dissolves. ASMOPSS loves asking exactly this kind of "does it have mass?" question.

What IS Matter? 🔍

Matter = anything with MASS (it can be weighed) AND VOLUME (it takes up space).

Rocks, water, air, your body, this screen — all matter. But some things are NOT matter, even though they're real and you can sense them:

🪨
IS matter
rock, water, air, wood, your breath (the air in it)
☀️
NOT matter
light, sound, heat, a shadow — no mass, no volume of their own
🔑 The test: ask "could I put this on a scale and get a weight?" You can weigh a cup of air. You can't weigh sunlight itself.

Three States of Matter 🧊💧💨

Same matter, different arrangement of tiny particles.
🧊
Solid
fixed shape
fixed volume
💧
Liquid
takes container's shape
fixed volume
💨
Gas
no fixed shape
no fixed volume — fills any space
Same rule for all three: whatever the state, it's still matter — it still has mass.

Gases Have Mass Too! 🎈

A puffed-up balloon is HEAVIER than a flat one — the air inside has mass!
Why: when you blow air into a balloon, you're packing real matter (gas) inside it. That gas has mass, so the full balloon weighs a tiny bit more than the empty one.
Same idea: a football pumped full of air weighs more than a flat, empty one — for exactly the same reason. Gas isn't "nothing" — it's matter with mass, just spread out and invisible.
