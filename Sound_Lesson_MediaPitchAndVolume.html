Quick Recall from Lesson 1 🔁

🔁 Sound-and-medium check: in Lesson 1 you learned that sound travels as vibrations passed from particle to particle, and that sound needs a medium (a solid, liquid, or gas) to travel through at all — no medium, no sound.

Here's the next question: if sound travels by bumping from particle to particle, does it matter how CLOSE TOGETHER those particles are? Today's lesson answers that — and it connects straight back to Unit 2's solid/liquid/gas particle-spacing picture.

Why learn this? 🤔

This is exactly why you can hear an approaching train by putting your ear to the metal rail long before you can hear it in the air, why whales can hear each other across entire oceans, why a doctor presses a stethoscope directly onto your skin instead of holding it in the air, and why a whisper and a shout can be the exact same pitch but very different volumes.
Real life: sonar operators use the speed of sound through water to measure ocean depth, musicians and audio engineers control pitch and volume separately on every instrument and speaker, and engineers use railway tracks as "sound conductors" for early train-detection.

How Fast Does Sound Travel? 🏎️

Sound travels FASTEST through SOLIDS, slower through LIQUIDS, and SLOWEST through GASES.

Remember Unit 2's particle picture? Solid particles are packed tightly together, so a vibration bumps from one particle to the next almost instantly. Liquid particles are looser, so it takes a little longer. Gas particles are spread far apart, so the vibration has to travel much further between each bump — making it the slowest of the three.

Key idea: the SAME sound travels through solid, liquid, and gas at three DIFFERENT speeds — solid fastest, liquid in the middle, gas slowest — because it all comes down to how close together the particles are.

Pitch vs Volume — Two Different Things! 🎵

PITCH is how HIGH or LOW a sound is. VOLUME is how LOUD or SOFT a sound is. They are completely separate — changing one does NOT automatically change the other.
🎹 Pitch (high ↔ low)
  • Comes from how FAST something vibrates
  • Fast vibration = HIGH pitch (e.g. a tiny bird tweet)
  • Slow vibration = LOW pitch (e.g. a deep drum)
🔊 Volume (loud ↔ soft)
  • Comes from how BIG/STRONG the vibration is
  • Big vibration = LOUD sound
  • Small vibration = SOFT (quiet) sound
Key idea: a sound can be high-pitched AND quiet (a soft whistle), high-pitched AND loud (a siren), low-pitched AND quiet (a soft hum), or low-pitched AND loud (a booming drum) — pitch and volume can mix in ANY combination.

Pitch Change or Volume Change? 🔍

Ask this one key question whenever a sound changes.
Did the sound become HIGHER/LOWER, or LOUDER/SOFTER?
HIGHER / LOWER
That's a PITCH change — the speed of the vibration changed.
LOUDER / SOFTER
That's a VOLUME change — the size/strength of the vibration changed.
Key idea: if a description mentions how "high" or "deep" a sound is, that's pitch. If it mentions how "loud" or "quiet" a sound is, that's volume — don't mix the two words up.

Worked Example: The Listening Train Driver 🧪

A railway worker wants to detect an approaching train as early as possible.

  1. Step 1: She knows sound travels through the metal rail (a solid) MUCH faster than through the air (a gas).
  2. Step 2: The train's wheels vibrate the metal rail as it approaches, long before the sound has travelled through the air to reach her ears directly.
  3. Step 3: She presses her ear against the rail. Since solids carry sound fastest, the vibration from the distant train reaches her ear through the rail well before the same sound would arrive through the air.
  4. Step 4: Prediction: she will hear the train through the rail before she can hear it in the air. She tests it — and she does, confirming the prediction.
🚫 Spot the flaw: A student claims: "Air is lighter than water, so sound must travel FASTER through air than through water." What's wrong with this claim?

Speed of sound doesn't depend on how "light" or "heavy" a material feels — it depends on how closely packed the particles are. Water's particles are much closer together than air's, so sound actually travels FASTER through water than through air, even though water feels "heavier." The student has the direction backwards.
