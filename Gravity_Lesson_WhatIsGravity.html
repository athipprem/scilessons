Quick Recall from Unit 10 🔁

🔁 Remember mass vs weight? Back in Unit 10 you learned that GRAVITY pulls every object toward the Earth, and that a book's MASS (the amount of matter in it) never changes — but its WEIGHT (the force gravity pulls on it with) would be smaller on the Moon, because the Moon's gravity is weaker than Earth's.

Today you'll use that exact idea again, but push it one step further: on the SAME planet, with the SAME gravity, does a heavier object get pulled down any harder, relative to its own mass, than a lighter one? The answer might surprise you.

Why learn this? 🤔

Understanding gravity and falling objects explains why parachutes work, why astronauts can do surprising experiments on the Moon, why raindrops don't hurt when they land on you, and why engineers have to think carefully about falling objects when designing safety equipment like bike helmets and car crumple zones.
Real life: skydivers and engineers who design parachutes need to know exactly how gravity and falling work together, so they can predict how fast something will fall and design equipment to slow it down safely. Space scientists have even tested gravity directly on the Moon, where the results are especially dramatic — you'll see exactly what they found in Lesson 2.

What Is Gravity? 🌍

GRAVITY is a pulling force between objects with mass. Earth's gravity pulls everything near it — you, a book, a feather, a car — straight down, toward the centre of the Earth.
Key idea: the bigger and heavier an object is, the harder gravity pulls it — but that object also has MORE mass to move, so the two effects cancel out perfectly. A bowling ball is pulled downward with a much bigger force than a tennis ball, but the bowling ball also has much more mass to accelerate, so it doesn't actually speed up any faster because of that stronger pull.

This is a strange idea, so let's see it directly on the next screen.

The Surprising Fact 🤯

If you drop a heavy ball and a light ball from the same height, at the same moment, and NOTHING else is acting on them (no air pushing back) — they hit the ground at EXACTLY the same time.
The heavy ball (dark, bigger) and the light ball (brown, smaller) are shown at four identical moments in time — notice they're always at the exact same height as each other. Dropped together with no air resistance, they fall together the whole way down and land at the same instant.
A famous story: legend says the scientist Galileo dropped two different-weight balls from the top of the Leaning Tower of Pisa in Italy, to test this very idea — and they landed together. Whether or not that exact story is true, the science behind it has been tested many times since and is completely correct.

Same Rate — But Only When...? 🌳

Is there air (or some other resistance) pushing back against the falling object?
↓
NO — nothing pushing back (a true vacuum, like deep space, or air resistance is negligible)
ALL objects fall at the SAME rate, no matter their mass
YES — air is pushing back
Shape and surface area start to matter too — find out how in Lesson 2!
Key idea: the "same rate for every mass" rule is always true for the PULL of gravity itself. But here on Earth, we almost never drop things in a true vacuum — we drop them through AIR, and air can push back differently on different shapes. That's exactly what Lesson 2 explains, using one of the most famous real experiments ever filmed.
