Quick Recall from Lesson 1 🧠

Speed = Distance ÷ Time. The same distance covered in less time means a higher speed. To compare two speeds, always calculate BOTH using this formula before comparing the numbers.
Example recap: A car travelling 100 km in 2 hours has a speed of 100 ÷ 2 = 50 km/h.
Today we go further: what if you're NOT given distance and time together? What if you're given speed and time, and need to find distance instead — or speed and distance, needing time? That's what the DST triangle is for.

Why learn this? 🤔

Real trips are rarely "here's the distance and time — find the speed." More often you know your speed limit and how far you're going, and need to work out how long the trip takes — or you know your speed and how long you can travel, and need to work out how far you'll get. Being able to rearrange the formula lets you answer ALL of these, not just one.
Real life: a pilot knows the plane's cruising speed and the distance to the destination — and uses exactly this rearranged formula to tell passengers "we'll arrive in 4 hours."

The DST Triangle 🔺

Cover up the letter you want to find — the other two show you exactly what to do.
How to read it: D sits on top, alone. S and T sit side by side on the bottom, split by a line.
• Want Distance? Cover the D — you see S next to T, meaning Distance = Speed × Time.
• Want Speed? Cover the S — you see D over T, meaning Speed = Distance ÷ Time.
• Want Time? Cover the T — you see D over S, meaning Time = Distance ÷ Speed.
Why it works: anything sitting side-by-side on the same line means MULTIPLY. Anything with one letter stacked above another means DIVIDE (top ÷ bottom). That's the whole trick.

Worked Examples ✏️

Finding Distance (cover the D → S × T): A truck drives at 60 km/h for 3 hours. Distance = Speed × Time = 60 × 3 = 180 km.
Finding Time (cover the T → D ÷ S): A plane needs to cover 800 km at a cruising speed of 400 km/h. Time = Distance ÷ Speed = 800 ÷ 400 = 2 hours.
Finding Speed (cover the S → D ÷ T): A ferry crosses 45 km of water in 3 hours. Speed = Distance ÷ Time = 45 ÷ 3 = 15 km/h.
Watch the units: if time is given in minutes but your speed is in km per HOUR, convert minutes to hours first (e.g. 30 minutes = 0.5 hours) before using the triangle — otherwise the answer will be wrong even though the method is right.

Average Speed — Not Just the Middle Number 🚌

If you travel part of a journey slow and part fast, your OVERALL average speed depends on how much TIME you actually spent at each speed — not just the two numbers averaged together.
Think it through: imagine cycling the first half of a route at 10 km/h, then the second half (the SAME distance) at 30 km/h. It's tempting to say "average = 20 km/h" (the exact middle of 10 and 30) — but that's wrong.
Why it's wrong: going 10 km/h is much slower, so that first half takes a LOT longer to cover than the second half at 30 km/h. Since you spend far MORE time riding at the slow speed, the slow speed "counts for more" in your overall average — pulling your true average speed DOWN, below the simple midpoint of 20 km/h.
The key idea (no tricky maths needed): whichever speed you spend MORE TIME travelling at pulls the average CLOSER to itself. Equal distances at very different speeds do NOT give equal time at each speed — so the average is never just the two numbers added and divided by two.

Which Formula Do I Need? 🌳

What is the question asking you to FIND?
Find DISTANCE (have speed & time)
Distance = Speed × Time
Find TIME (have distance & speed)
Time = Distance ÷ Speed
Find SPEED (have distance & time)
Speed = Distance ÷ Time
How to use this: underline what the question is asking you to FIND first, then check which two values you already have — the triangle tells you exactly what to do with them. Always check your units match (hours with km/h, seconds with m/s) before calculating.
