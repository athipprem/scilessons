Why learn this? 🤔

Knowing whether forces are balanced or not explains why a parked car stays still, why a book doesn't sink through a table, and why brakes work. Understanding friction explains why some shoes grip better than others, why ice is dangerous to walk on, and why race cars use special tyres.
Real life: engineers design tyre treads to increase friction with the road for a better grip, especially in the rain. Furniture makers add rubber pads under chair legs to increase friction so they don't slide. Car designers shape vehicles to reduce air resistance so they use less fuel.

Balanced vs Unbalanced Forces ⚖️

When forces on an object are BALANCED, its motion does NOT change. When forces are UNBALANCED, its motion DOES change.
Key idea: a book resting on a table looks like nothing is happening, but TWO forces are actually acting on it: gravity pulling it down, and the table pushing back up. Because these two forces are exactly equal and opposite, they're BALANCED — the book stays perfectly still. If you then push the book sideways, that adds a new, unbalanced force, and the book starts to move.

Balanced or Unbalanced? 🔍

Use this checklist to decide whether the forces on an object are balanced.

NO — stays still or steady

Key idea: a tug-of-war rope that isn't moving looks like "nothing is happening," but both teams ARE pulling hard — the forces are just balanced (equal and opposite), so the rope stays still. The moment one team pulls harder than the other, the forces become unbalanced and the rope starts to move.

Gravity & Friction ⬇️

GRAVITY pulls every object toward the Earth. FRICTION is a force between two touching surfaces that opposes (works against) motion.
Key idea: gravity is why a dropped pencil falls and why we all stay on the ground instead of floating away. On the Moon, gravity is much weaker, so a book WEIGHS less there — but it still has exactly the same MASS (the same amount of matter), a distinction you first met back in Unit 2. Friction is different: rougher surfaces create MORE friction (more force opposing motion), which is why a ball rolls much farther on smooth tile than on rough gravel.

Think Like a Scientist 🔬

FAIR TEST RULE: change only ONE thing at a time, and keep everything else exactly the same, so you know what actually caused any difference you see.
Worked example — testing which surface has MORE friction:
  1. Take one toy car and one ramp of a fixed height.
  2. Release the car from the SAME point on the ramp every time, with the SAME starting push (or no push at all — just let it roll).
  3. Change only ONE thing: the surface the car rolls onto at the bottom (first smooth tile, then carpet, then rough gravel).
  4. Measure how far the car travels before stopping on each surface.
  5. Because the surface was the ONLY thing different, any difference in how far the car travelled can be fairly blamed on the surface's friction — not on the ramp height or the car's starting push, since those were kept identical.
CHANGED
the surface the car rolls onto
= the one variable being tested
MEASURED
distance travelled before stopping
= the result we're comparing
KEPT SAME
ramp height, starting point, the car itself
= everything else, so the test is fair
Spot the flawed claim: a student says "My car travelled the FARTHEST on smooth tile, so tile must have the MOST friction." Is this correct? (No — travelling FARTHER means LESS friction was slowing the car down, not more. The surface with the most friction is the one where the car stops soonest — rough gravel — not the one where it travels farthest.)

1. A ball sits still on flat ground. What is TRUE about the forces acting on it?

✔ Right — gravity pulling down and the ground pushing up are equal and opposite, so the ball stays still.
✘ Think about the book-on-a-table example. What two forces are always present, even when something looks still?

2. A skateboard is rolling steadily along flat ground, then someone gives it a push from behind and it speeds up. What just happened to the forces?

✔ Correct — the extra push unbalanced the forces, which is exactly why the speed changed.
✘ Recall the checklist: forces are unbalanced whenever motion actually changes. What changed here?

3. A ball is rolled with the same push onto smooth tile and, separately, onto rough gravel. Which travels FARTHER before stopping?

✔ Exactly — less friction means less force opposing the motion, so the ball keeps rolling farther.
✘ Which surface creates MORE friction, and does more friction help or oppose motion?

4. A book has a mass of 1 kg on Earth. If the same book were taken to the Moon, which is TRUE?

✔ Correct — mass (amount of matter) doesn't change, but weight (the force of gravity on it) does, since gravity is weaker on the Moon.
✘ Remember the Unit 2 distinction: mass is the amount of matter; weight is a FORCE caused by gravity. Which one depends on how strong gravity is?

5. A student sets up a fair test to compare friction on two surfaces but uses a different ramp height for each surface. What is wrong with this test?

✔ Correct — a fair test changes only ONE variable; changing both surface and ramp height makes the result impossible to interpret fairly.
✘ A fair test isolates ONE variable. How many things were different between the two setups here?
