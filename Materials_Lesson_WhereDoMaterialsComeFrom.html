Quick Recall from Unit 5 🔁

🔁 States of Matter & Materials check: back in Unit 5, you sorted materials by their PROPERTIES — absorbent or waterproof, hard or soft.

Today we sort materials a completely different way: not by what they're like, but by where they actually came from before they became the object in front of you. The same material can even show up in both kinds of sorting — rubber is soft AND it comes from a plant!

Why learn this? 🤔

Knowing where materials come from helps you sort recycling correctly, choose more sustainable products (like bamboo instead of plastic), understand allergies (wool and latex rubber can trigger them), and make sense of where everyday objects around your home actually began.
Real life: recycling centres separate waste partly by origin and material type so it can be reused properly. Farmers and factories that grow cotton or raise sheep for wool are part of huge global industries. Historically, silk and rubber were so valuable that entire trade routes and economies were built around moving them around the world.

Meet the Four Origins 🌍

Every material's ORIGIN is where it came from before it was shaped into the object you see.
🌳 Plant materials
wood, cotton, rubber (tree sap), paper
grown by a living plant
🐑 Animal materials
wool, leather, silk, feathers
produced by a living animal
⛏️ Ground & mineral materials
sand, clay, metal ore, rock, coal
dug or mined from the ground
🏭 Man-made materials
plastic, nylon, polyester
chemically manufactured by people
Key idea: the first three groups (plant, animal, ground) all exist in nature already — people just collect, shape, or process them. Man-made materials are different: people chemically build them from scratch, often starting with oil or other chemicals.

Watch Out — Tricky Origins! ⚠️

These two materials trick almost everyone the first time — read carefully!
🌳 RUBBER
Feels stretchy and synthetic, like something from a factory... but it actually comes from the milky sap (called LATEX) of rubber trees. That makes it a PLANT material, not man-made and not "ground."
🐛 SILK
Looks like a woven fabric, similar to cotton (a plant material)... but silk thread is actually spun by SILKWORMS from their cocoons. That makes it an ANIMAL material, not a plant material.
Key idea: don't judge a material's origin just by how it LOOKS or FEELS — always ask "what did this actually come from?" Wool (from sheep) and cotton (from a plant) can feel similar too, but one is animal and the other is plant.

Where Does This Material Come From? 🔍

Follow these questions in order, one at a time, to find any material's true origin.
Did a LIVING PLANT produce this directly — its sap, fibres, or wood?
↓ NO
Did an ANIMAL produce this — fur, hide, silk, or feathers?
↓ NO
Was this dug or mined from the GROUND, mostly unchanged — sand, clay, ore, rock?
↓ NO
It must be MAN-MADE — chemically manufactured by people (e.g. plastic, nylon).
Key idea: as soon as you get a YES to any question, stop — that's the origin group. Rubber gets a YES on the very first question (a plant's sap), so you never even need to ask the later questions.
