Why learn this? 🤔

Knowing whether a change can be undone matters in real life — you can re-freeze melted ice cream, but you can never un-bake a cake back into flour, eggs, and sugar. Knowing what speeds reactions up (like temperature) explains why food spoils faster in a warm kitchen than in a fridge, and why crushed medicine tablets work faster than whole ones.
Real life: chefs use heat to speed up cooking reactions on purpose. Doctors know crushed tablets can act faster than whole ones because more surface is exposed. Engineers coat iron and steel in paint or oil specifically to slow down the (irreversible) rusting reaction.

Reversible vs Irreversible Changes 🔄

A REVERSIBLE change can be undone, getting the original substance back. An IRREVERSIBLE change cannot easily be undone.
REVERSIBLE
melting → freezing, dissolving → evaporating, folding paper
mostly physical changes
IRREVERSIBLE
burning wood, rusting iron, baking a cake, cooking an egg
mostly chemical reactions
Key idea: most physical changes are reversible — you can melt ice and freeze it right back into ice. Most chemical reactions are irreversible in everyday life — once an egg is cooked, no amount of cooling turns it back into raw egg, because the reaction created genuinely new substances.
Watch out: reversible doesn't always mean "easy" — you'd need a special process to reverse some reactions in a lab. For everyday science, though, if you can't undo it with a simple physical step (freezing, evaporating, reshaping), treat it as irreversible.

↓
YES
REVERSIBLE change
e.g. melted ice, dissolved sugar, bent wire
NO
IRREVERSIBLE change
e.g. burnt toast, rusted nail, baked cake
Key idea: toast browning in a toaster is a chemical reaction between the bread's sugars and heat — you can never turn toast back into plain bread, so it's irreversible. Melted butter on that toast, though, is just a physical change — pop it in the fridge and it turns solid again.

What Makes a Reaction Speed Up or Slow Down? ⚡

Three things generally speed a reaction up: more HEAT (temperature), a bigger SURFACE AREA (crushed/broken up), and a higher CONCENTRATION (amount of reacting substance).
Key idea: warmer temperatures give particles more energy, so they react faster and produce gas quicker — that's why baking soda fizzes faster in warm vinegar than cold. Crushing a tablet into powder exposes more surface area to react at once, so it dissolves and reacts faster than a whole tablet. Whichever of these you change, the reaction itself is still the same reaction — just faster or slower.

Think Like a Scientist 🔬

FAIR TEST RULE: change only ONE thing at a time, and keep everything else exactly the same, so you know what actually caused any difference you see.
Worked example — testing whether TEMPERATURE affects reaction speed:
  1. Take two identical cups, each with the same amount of vinegar.
  2. Give both the SAME amount of baking soda, added at the SAME time.
  3. Change only ONE thing: warm the vinegar in Cup A first; keep Cup B's vinegar cold.
  4. Time how long the fizzing lasts (or how much gas is made) in each cup.
  5. Because temperature was the ONLY thing different, any difference in fizzing speed can be fairly blamed on the temperature — not on the amount of vinegar or baking soda, since those were kept identical.
CHANGED
the temperature of the vinegar
= the one variable being tested
MEASURED
fizzing time / gas produced
= the result we're comparing
KEPT SAME
amount of vinegar, amount of baking soda, timing
= everything else, so the test is fair
Spot the flawed claim: a student says "Cup A fizzed for longer AND I used a bigger spoon of baking soda in Cup A too, so temperature must be what made the difference." Is this a fair test? (No — TWO things changed at once, the temperature and the amount of baking soda, so you can't tell which one actually caused the longer fizzing.)
