Why learn this? 🤔

Understanding breathing and gas exchange explains why you breathe faster after running, why stuffy rooms feel hard to breathe in, why mountain climbers need oxygen tanks at high altitude, and why opening a window in a crowded room actually helps.
Real life: submarine crews carefully monitor oxygen and carbon dioxide levels because they're sealed inside for long periods. Building engineers design ventilation systems to keep offices and classrooms full of fresh air. Doctors measure oxygen levels in blood to check how well the lungs are working.

How Breathing Works 🫁

When you breathe IN, your lungs fill with air. When you breathe OUT, air is pushed back out.
Key idea: air travels down your windpipe, which splits into two tubes — one for each lung — and into your lungs, which are full of millions of tiny air sacs. At each air sac, oxygen from the air you breathed in passes into your blood, and carbon dioxide (a waste gas from your cells) passes out of your blood into the air — this swap is called GAS EXCHANGE. (Notice the left lung is a little smaller with a dip in its shape — that's to leave room for your heart, which sits just left of centre in your chest.)

Fresh Air vs. Air You Breathe Out 💨

The air you breathe OUT is different from the air you breathe IN — gas exchange changes it.
Air breathed IN
more oxygen
less carbon dioxide
drier
Air breathed OUT
less oxygen (some is used up)
more carbon dioxide (a waste gas)
more water vapour (moister)
Key idea: you don't use up ALL the oxygen you breathe in — exhaled air still has some oxygen left, just less than fresh air. And it's not just carbon dioxide that increases — the air you breathe out is also more moist (more water vapour) than the air you breathed in, because your lungs add moisture to it.

Key idea: every breath removes a little oxygen from the air and adds carbon dioxide and water vapour back into it. With no fresh air coming in to replace what's used up, these changes build up more and more the longer people stay inside.

Think Like a Scientist 🔬

WORKED EXAMPLE: predicting gas changes in a sealed minivan.
Scenario: Five people sit inside a parked minivan with all the windows and doors shut for 40 minutes, on a cold morning, waiting for school to start.
  1. Five people are breathing continuously the whole time.
  2. No windows are open, so no fresh air is coming in and no stale air is going out.
  3. Every breath taken removes a little oxygen from the air inside the van and adds carbon dioxide and water vapour.
  4. Because nothing is refreshing the air, these small changes ADD UP over the full 40 minutes.
  5. Prediction: by the end, the air inside the van should have LESS oxygen, MORE carbon dioxide, and MORE water vapour (the windows may even fog up!) than when they first got in.
Spot the flawed claim: a student says "the air inside will have MORE oxygen after 40 minutes, because breathing adds air into the van." Is this correct? (No — breathing doesn't add fresh air into a sealed van from outside; it only recycles the air that's already trapped inside, using up oxygen and adding carbon dioxide and water vapour each time.)
