Quick Recall from Unit 2 🔁

🔁 Mass & volume check: back in Unit 2, you learned that MASS is how much matter is inside something (measured in grams), and VOLUME is how much SPACE something takes up (measured in cm³ or mL) — and that these are two completely different things, even though people sometimes mix them up.

Here's the connection: density combines mass AND volume together. It tells you how much mass is squeezed into a certain amount of space. Today you'll learn exactly how to work that out and compare it between objects.

Why learn this? 🤔

Understanding density explains why a huge steel ship can float while a tiny pebble sinks, why a cork bobs on top of water but a coin drops straight to the bottom, why cooking oil always floats on top of water, and why a helium balloon rises into the sky while a balloon full of regular air just sits there.
Real life: shipbuilders use density to design hulls that float, divers use weighted belts to control whether they sink or rise, and scientists use density to identify unknown materials — density is one of the most useful "detective clues" in all of science.

What Does "Density" Actually Mean? ⚖️

DENSITY = how tightly packed an object's mass is, squeezed into its volume (its size).

Imagine two boxes that are EXACTLY the same size on the outside. If you fill one box with feathers and the other box with the same-sized space full of rocks, the rock-box will have far more mass squeezed into the same amount of space. That means the rock-box is denser than the feather-box.

Two simple rules for comparing density:
1. If two objects are the SAME SIZE (same volume), the one with MORE MASS is denser.
2. If two objects have the SAME MASS, the one that is SMALLER (less volume) is denser, because its mass is squeezed into less space.

Ranking Objects by Density 🔢

When several objects are all the SAME SIZE, you can rank them by density just by comparing their masses.

Worked example: Four balls are all exactly the same size (same volume). Let's rank them from LEAST dense to MOST dense.

Step by step: Since all four balls are the SAME SIZE, the LIGHTEST ball has the LEAST density, and the HEAVIEST ball has the MOST density. Just sort the masses from smallest to largest: 60g → 90g → 150g → 200g. So the order from least to most dense is P, R, Q, S.
💡 This exact skill — ranking same-sized objects by mass to find density order — is a favourite competition question style. Whenever objects are described as "the same size," density ranking is really just mass ranking.

Which Object Is Denser? 🔍

Ask this one key question whenever you're comparing two objects' density.
Are the two objects the SAME SIZE (same volume)?
YES
Compare their MASS — whichever one is HEAVIER is denser.
NO
If they have the SAME MASS instead, whichever one is SMALLER is denser. (If neither size nor mass matches, you'd need to actually measure both to compare.)
Key idea: density comparisons are easiest when one of the two properties (size OR mass) is already the same between the two objects — then you only need to compare the other one.
