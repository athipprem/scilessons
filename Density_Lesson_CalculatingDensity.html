Quick Fix: When a Fair Test Shows NO Difference 💡

A fair test that shows NO difference between two groups is NOT a broken test — it's a real, useful answer, just like any other result.

Think about flipping coins. Imagine you flip an ordinary coin 20 times, and a friend flips their "lucky" coin 20 times. Both coins land on heads about the same number of times — roughly 10 each.

It's tempting to think: "well, nothing proved anything, so this test didn't work." But that's backwards. The test DID work — and it proved something important: the "lucky" coin isn't actually any luckier than a normal coin. If the lucky coin really did have some special power, you'd expect it to land on heads noticeably MORE often. It didn't. That "no difference" result is the whole answer to the question you were asking.

The wrong instinct to watch for: when an experiment shows no difference, it's tempting to explain it away — "maybe I did it wrong" or "maybe there's a difference that's just too small to see." Sometimes that's true! But often, "no difference" simply means the thing you changed genuinely doesn't make a difference under those conditions — and that itself is valuable, real information. Proving nothing changed is still proving something.

Quick Check 1: A student flips a normal coin 30 times and a "special" coin 30 times. Both coins land heads almost exactly half the time. What is the BEST conclusion?

✔ Right — the test worked, and "no difference" is itself the useful, real answer.
✘ Remember: a fair test showing no difference is still a valid, working test — it just answered "no" instead of "yes."

Quick Check 2: Two identical toy cars are rolled down two identical ramps — one ramp is painted red, the other blue. Both cars roll exactly the same distance every time. What does this experiment tell you?

✔ Correct — since only the colour changed and the distance stayed the same, colour genuinely has no effect here. That's a real, useful finding.
✘ Only ONE thing changed (the colour) and the result stayed the same both times — that IS a real, informative answer, not a failed test.

Quick Recall from Unit 15 🔁

🔁 Remember density shortcuts? In Unit 15 you learned that DENSITY is how tightly packed an object's mass is, squeezed into its volume. You also learned two shortcuts: if two objects are the SAME SIZE, the one with MORE mass is denser. If two objects have the SAME MASS, the smaller one is denser.

Those shortcuts are handy — but what if two objects are DIFFERENT sizes AND different masses? The shortcuts don't work anymore. Today you'll learn the actual formula that works for absolutely any object, every time.

Why learn this? 🤔

The exact density formula is what lets scientists identify an unknown material just by weighing it and measuring its size — gold has a very specific density, so does aluminium, so does wood. It's also how engineers work out exactly how much cargo a ship can carry before it sinks, and how jewellers can tell real gold from a fake without cutting it open.
Real life: archaeologists use density calculations to check whether an ancient coin is genuine gold or a cheaper metal painted to look like gold, and recycling plants use density to automatically sort different types of plastic that all look identical.

The Density Formula ➗

Density = Mass ÷ Volume

This one formula works for ANY object, no matter its size or mass — you don't need a shortcut, and you don't need the two objects to share anything in common. Density is usually measured in grams per cubic centimetre (g/cm³) — that just means "how many grams of mass are packed into each single cm³ of space."

Worked example: A metal block has a mass of 270g and a volume of 30cm³. What is its density?

  1. Step 1: Write down the formula: Density = Mass ÷ Volume.
  2. Step 2: Put in the numbers: Density = 270g ÷ 30cm³.
  3. Step 3: Divide: 270 ÷ 30 = 9.
  4. Step 4: Add the units: the block's density is 9 g/cm³.
Key idea: the formula divides mass BY volume — mass always goes first (on top), volume always goes second (on the bottom). Mixing up the order gives a completely wrong answer, even if you use the right numbers.

Comparing Objects the Formula Can Handle 🧮

When two objects are DIFFERENT sizes AND different masses, the Unit 15 shortcuts don't work — you must calculate both densities and then compare the numbers.

Worked example: Block A has a mass of 160g and a volume of 20cm³. Block B has a mass of 300g and a volume of 50cm³. Which block is denser?

  1. Step 1: Calculate Block A's density: 160 ÷ 20 = 8 g/cm³.
  2. Step 2: Calculate Block B's density: 300 ÷ 50 = 6 g/cm³.
  3. Step 3: Compare the two density NUMBERS (not the masses, and not the volumes on their own): 8 g/cm³ is bigger than 6 g/cm³.
  4. Step 4: Block A is denser, even though Block B has the bigger mass — because Block A's mass is packed into far less space.
Key idea: this is exactly why the shortcuts from Unit 15 only work in SPECIAL cases — Block B has more mass, but that alone doesn't make it denser once the volumes are different too. The formula is the only method that always works, for every pair of objects.

Shortcut, or Calculate? 🌳

Do the two objects share the SAME size OR the SAME mass?
YES — one of the two matches
Use the Unit 15 shortcut (compare the one that's different)
NO — both size and mass are different
Calculate BOTH densities with Mass ÷ Volume, then compare the two numbers
How to use this: always check first whether a shortcut applies — it's faster. But whenever both size and mass are different between two objects, the formula is the only reliable way to compare them.
