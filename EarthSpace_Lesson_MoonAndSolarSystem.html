Why learn this? 🤔

The Moon has guided calendars, tides, and night-time travel for thousands of years — and its changing shape in the sky is one of the most obvious things anyone can observe about space without a telescope. Understanding WHY it changes shape (and that Earth has seven other planet-neighbours orbiting the same Sun) is core "big picture" science.
Real life: fishing communities and farmers have tracked Moon phases for centuries to predict tides and plan work. Space agencies plan Moon missions around specific phases too, since lighting conditions on the Moon's surface depend on where the Sun is shining.

The Moon: Reflected Light, Not Its Own ✨

The Moon has NO light of its own. What you see is SUNLIGHT bouncing off the Moon's surface, the same way sunlight bounces off a mirror or a wall.
Key fact: the Moon orbits (travels around) Earth, taking about 27–29 days for one full trip. As it orbits, the half of the Moon facing the Sun is always lit — but how much of THAT lit half we can see from Earth keeps changing, because the Moon's position relative to Earth and the Sun keeps changing.
Common mix-up: some people think Earth's shadow "covers up" parts of the Moon to make the phases. That's actually what causes a rare LUNAR ECLIPSE, not the everyday phases you see almost every night. Regular phases are about VIEWING ANGLE, not shadow.

Why the Moon's Shape Seems to Change 🌓

The phase you see depends on WHERE the Moon is in its orbit around Earth, compared to the Sun.

Key idea: the Moon itself never changes — only how much of its permanently-lit half we can see from Earth changes, as it moves around its orbit.

Our Solar System: The Basic Layout ☀️🪐

The SUN is a star at the centre. PLANETS (including Earth) orbit the Sun. MOONS orbit planets — Earth has one Moon, but some other planets have many.
☀️ The Sun
a star — makes its own light and heat
🪐 Planets
orbit the Sun — Earth is one of eight
🌙 Moons
orbit planets, not the Sun directly
🔭 Order matters
everything orbits something bigger: moons→planets→Sun
Key idea: Earth orbits the Sun (that's revolution, from Lesson 1). The Moon orbits EARTH, not the Sun directly. Two different orbits, two different centres.

Think Like a Scientist 🔬

MODEL RULE: when something is too big, too far away, or too slow to test directly, scientists build a smaller MODEL that behaves the same way and study that instead.

You can't fly to the Moon to check why its shape seems to change — but you CAN model it: hold a ball (the Moon) at arm's length, with a lamp (the Sun) lighting it from one side, and your head as Earth. Slowly walk the ball around your head.

🧠 What the model shows: as the ball moves around your head, you see different amounts of its lit side — just like real Moon phases. When the ball is between you and the lamp, its lit side faces away from you (New Moon). When it's on the far side, its lit side faces you fully (Full Moon).
Spot the flawed claim: a student says "Moon phases happen because Earth's shadow covers different parts of the Moon each night." Is this correct? (No — that describes a lunar ECLIPSE, a rare event. Regular phases are caused by the CHANGING VIEWING ANGLE as the Moon orbits, not by Earth's shadow.)
